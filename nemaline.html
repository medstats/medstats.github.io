Hello, Chief Resident here.

This is a complex presentation involving progressive cardiopulmonary decline and new-onset esophageal symptoms in a patient with established Antiphospholipid Antibody Syndrome (APS) and long-term exposure to Clozapine.

Key Findings:

  1. Progressive Dyspnea (22 months): Initially exertional, now worsening.
  2. Restrictive Lung Disease: PFTs show significant restriction (TLC 63%) and a disproportionately severe reduction in DLCO (26%).
  3. Vascular Pathology: History of extensive acute bilateral PE and RV dilatation, with confirmed APS (triple positivity or high-titer LAC).
  4. New Dysphagia: Suggests either a systemic process (connective tissue disease) or a drug-induced complication.
  5. Medication Profile: Long-term Clozapine (500mg/day), which carries a black-box warning for serositis and cardiomyopathy.

Clinical Analysis:

The severe reduction in DLCO relative to the restrictive defect suggests a primary pulmonary vascular process (Chronic Thromboembolic Pulmonary Hypertension - CTEPH) or an interstitial process. However, the combination of restrictive lung disease, dysphagia, and APS strongly points toward an underlying Systemic Sclerosis (Scleroderma) or a drug-induced fibrotic process. Clozapine is rarely associated with drug-induced serositis and polyserositis, but more importantly, it can cause gastrointestinal hypomotility (clozapine-induced gastrointestinal hypomotility - CIGH), though this usually presents as constipation rather than dysphagia.

Differential Diagnoses:

  1. Systemic Sclerosis (Scleroderma) - Limited or Diffuse (45%): This explains the restrictive lung disease (ILD), the severe DLCO reduction (Pulmonary Arterial Hypertension), the dysphagia (esophageal dysmotility), and the presence of APS/prothrombotic state which can overlap with CTDs.
  2. Chronic Thromboembolic Pulmonary Hypertension (CTEPH) (30%): Given the history of extensive PE and APS, persistent vascular obstruction is highly likely. This explains the RV dilatation and low DLCO, though it doesn't fully account for the dysphagia unless there is massive pulmonary artery enlargement causing cardiovocal syndrome or esophageal compression (rare).
  3. Clozapine-Induced Polyserositis/Fibrosis (15%): Clozapine has been linked to pleuritis, pericarditis, and rarely, pulmonary fibrosis. The dysphagia could be a separate side effect (CIGH or pharyngeal dysfunction).
  4. Antiphospholipid Syndrome-Associated Nephropathy/Microangiopathy (10%): While usually renal, APS can cause a "shrinking lung syndrome" or chronic microvascular occlusion leading to the observed PFTs.

Recommended Next Steps & Prioritised Tests:

  1. High-Resolution CT (HRCT) of the Chest: To differentiate between Interstitial Lung Disease (ILD) and signs of Pulmonary Hypertension (e.g., PA diameter).
  2. Echocardiogram with Bubble Study: To assess current RV function, estimate PASP, and look for signs of chronic pressure overload.
  3. Autoantibody Screen: Specifically ANA (with pattern), Anti-Scl-70, Anti-centromere, and Anti-RNA Polymerase III to evaluate for Scleroderma.
  4. V/Q Scan: The gold standard to screen for CTEPH, as it is more sensitive than CTPA for chronic webs and stenoses.
  5. Barium Swallow or Esophageal Manometry: To characterize the dysphagia (e.g., "scleroderma esophagus" vs. drug-induced dysmotility).

Chief Resident's Question: Does the patient have any cutaneous findings (sclerodactyly, telangiectasia, or Raynaud's phenomenon) or any history of pericarditis/pleuritis?

In SLONM with monoclonal gammopathy, outcomes are best when treatment is directed at the plasma cell/monoclonal clone, rather than treating it like routine inflammatory myositis alone.

Typical approaches include:
- autologous stem cell transplantation (ASCT) in eligible patients
- or plasma cell–directed chemotherapy such as bortezomib-based regimens

Supportive/bridging therapy may include:
- IVIG in selected cases
- respiratory support
- nutrition/swallow support

Most important parallel management issue

Do not lose sight of the pulmonary hypertension

She still has:
- severe precapillary PH
- RV failure/shock physiology
- APS with prior major PE

So parallel urgent management remains:
- expert CTEPH evaluation
- lifelong APS-appropriate anticoagulation, usually warfarin
- pulmonary hypertension center care
- assessment for endarterectomy or balloon pulmonary angioplasty if CTEPH is confirmed

Why this case is so instructive

Because SLONM can masquerade as:
- inflammatory myopathy
- myasthenia
- deconditioning
- CTD overlap disease

And it often has:
- normal CK
- bulbar symptoms
- respiratory weakness
- monoclonal gammopathy

Those clues should trigger biopsy consideration early.

Teaching pearl

In an adult with progressive proximal and neck weakness, bulbar symptoms, normal CK, and a monoclonal gammopathy, think SLONM and obtain a muscle biopsy—because treatment is often clone-directed, not standard myositis therapy.
