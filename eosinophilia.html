Round 1

User: A 64-year-old man was brought to the emergency department for sudden-onset blurred vision in both eyes and diplopia, along with a frontal headache. On presentation, he had an altered mental status. His visual symptoms spontaneously resolved shortly after arrival. He had no fevers, chills, or focal weakness. His medical history includes hypertension, chronic kidney disease (CKD), and an abdominal aortic aneurysm repaired 7 years prior. His only medications were atenolol and aspirin. He worked as a truck driver. On physical examination, the patient was lethargic and oriented to person and place, but not to time, with difficulty in word finding. He was afebrile. BP was 145/90 mmHg, Pulse 72 bpm, RR 14 bpm, and SpO2 95% on room air. Chest auscultation revealed scattered bilateral end-inspiratory crackles. Heart sounds were normal with no murmurs. Abdominal exam was normal. Several splinter hemorrhages were noted in the nail beds of both hands and the left foot. Neurologically, he had an ataxic gait and mild dysmetria, but strength and sensation were normal. Initial labs showed a leukocyte count (TLC) of 10,300/mm³ with a striking 59% eosinophilia (Absolute Eosinophil Count ~6000/mm³). Hb was 11.8 g/dL and Platelets were 234,000/mm³. Serum creatinine was elevated at 2.5 mg/dL (no baseline available). LFT showed an AST of 62 U/L and ALP of 111 U/L, with normal bilirubin. Urinalysis showed 1+ protein with no cells or casts. Toxicology was positive for amphetamines and cannabinoids. A chest radiograph revealed bilateral apical opacities.

Resident:

Hello, Chief Resident here.

Key findings include sudden transient visual loss, encephalopathy, ataxia, and splinter hemorrhages in a patient with a history of aortic aneurysm repair. Most striking is the profound peripheral eosinophilia (6000/mm³), acute kidney injury, and apical lung opacities.

The constellation of multisystem involvement (CNS, skin, kidneys) following vascular history suggests an embolic or systemic inflammatory process.

Differential Diagnoses:

  1. Cholesterol Embolization Syndrome (CES) (50%): The "blue toe" equivalent (splinter hemorrhages), CKD progression, and eosinophilia (seen in 60-80% of acute cases) following aortic pathology are classic.
  2. Eosinophilic Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (EGPA) (25%): Explains the asthma-like lung opacities, high eosinophilia, and multi-organ involvement (mononeuritis/CNS).
  3. Hypereosinophilic Syndrome (HES) (15%): Can cause thromboembolic events and encephalopathy, though usually more chronic.
  4. Infective Endocarditis (10%): Splinter hemorrhages and CNS symptoms fit, though eosinophilia is atypical.

Recommended Next Steps:
1. Skin/Renal Biopsy: To confirm cholesterol crystals or vasculitis.
2. Echocardiogram (TTE/TEE): Evaluate for vegetations or intracardiac thrombus.
3. MRI Brain: Assess for multifocal embolic infarcts.
4. ANCA Serology: To screen for EGPA.
5. Complement levels (C3/C4): Often low in CES.
