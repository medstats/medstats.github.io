Critical Appraisal Workbench

An opinionated journal-club worksheet based on the MSCA appraisal slide skill: decide whether a paper should change practice, not merely whether it is interesting.

Journal Club Question Validity Effects Bottom Line

1. Frame the Study Before You Judge It

Start with PICO/PECO, design, time horizon, and clinical need. Missing details should be labelled "not reported / unclear".

2. Fast Red Flags

Any major red flag should pull the conclusion toward "do not change practice" or "needs confirmatory evidence".

3. Design-Specific Questions

The prompts change with study design. Use them to prevent generic appraisal.

4. Three-Pillar Appraisal

Score lightly, but argue in words. A score never replaces narrative judgement.

Trustworthiness

Randomisation/confounding, concealment, blinding, missing data, reporting.

Applicability

Representativeness, comparator relevance, access, cost, monitoring, local fit.

Clinical Meaning

Absolute benefit, harms, precision, patient-important outcomes, feasibility.

5. Effect Size and Precision

Prefer absolute effects. Do not convert a hazard ratio directly into NNT unless valid absolute risk over a defined horizon is available.

Binary event calculator: enter event counts only when the outcome is a clean 2 x 2 event table. For time-to-event endpoints, use trial-reported absolute risks at a defined time point.
Enter counts to calculate EER, CER, absolute difference, RR, and NNT/NNH.

6. RoB 2, GRADE, and Reporting Discipline

Use conservative judgement. Overall risk of bias should not be better than the worst serious domain without a clear reason.

7. Practice Bottom Line

Choose the action first, then defend it. This is the slide-skill’s core habit.
