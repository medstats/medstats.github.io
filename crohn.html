Round 1

User: A 54-year-old man presented to a primary care clinic with a two-year history of worsening lower back pain. The pain was sudden in onset, sharp, constant, and non-radiating, occurring in the absence of trauma. Over the last six months, the pain had become severe enough to frequently awaken him from sleep. He reported morning stiffness lasting one to two hours, which was partially relieved by activity and non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs). Over the past year, the pain had expanded to include his neck, right shoulder, and sternum. He also reported significant fatigue and weakness in his arms and legs, necessitating the use of a cane and an abdominal binder for walking. His medical history was significant for type 2 diabetes mellitus, hypertension, and gastroesophageal reflux disease. He was born in Korea and immigrated to the United States at age 26, continuing to travel to Korea annually. He was a current smoker with a 30 pack-year history.

Resident:

Hello, Chief Resident here.

Key findings include chronic, progressive inflammatory back pain (nocturnal awakening, morning stiffness, relief with activity) in a 54-year-old male smoker with Korean heritage. The expansion to the sternum and peripheral joints, combined with significant weakness requiring mobility aids, suggests a systemic process affecting the axial skeleton and potentially the musculature or bone density.

Differential Diagnoses:
1. Ankylosing Spondylitis (AS) / Axial Spondyloarthritis: 45% – Classic inflammatory pattern, sternal involvement (enthesitis), and prolonged morning stiffness.
2. Multiple Myeloma: 25% – Constant pain, nocturnal awakening, and weakness in an older patient; must rule out plasma cell dyscrasia.
3. Metastatic Disease (e.g., Lung Cancer): 15% – 30 pack-year history and progressive axial pain; however, the 2-year duration is long for untreated malignancy.
4. Tuberculous Spondylitis (Pott’s Disease): 10% – Frequent travel to Korea; chronic indolent course.
5. Osteomalacia (Oncogenic or Nutritional): 5% – Can present with diffuse bone pain and proximal weakness.

Recommended Tests:
1. MRI Spine and Sacroiliac Joints: To evaluate for sacroiliitis or marrow-replacing lesions.
2. HLA-B27: To support a spondyloarthritis diagnosis.
3. Serum/Urine Protein Electrophoresis (SPEP/UPEP) with Free Light Chains: To screen for myeloma.
4. ESR and CRP: To quantify systemic inflammation.
5. CT Chest/Abdomen/Pelvis: To screen for malignancy or lymphadenopathy.

Attending:

Hello Attending Consultant here.

Top 3 Differential Diagnoses:
1. Axial spondyloarthritis (AS/axSpA) with enthesitis – classic inflammatory nocturnal pain, prolonged morning stiffness, sternal involvement, partial NSAID response, HLA-B27-associated demographics.
2. Multiple myeloma – age-appropriate, constant non-radiating axial pain waking from sleep, progressive fatigue/weakness, possible lytic bone disease.
3. Metastatic prostate or lung carcinoma – smoking history, insidious axial progression, constitutional symptoms; less likely given 2-year duration without focal neurology.

Suggestions (5 points):
- Order urgent whole-spine MRI with STIR sequences plus sacroiliac views.
- Obtain HLA-B27, ESR/CRP, SPEP/UPEP/free light chains, PSA.
- Perform full neurologic exam and consider EMG for weakness etiology.
- Initiate smoking cessation counseling and DEXA scan.
- Rheumatology referral within 2 weeks; avoid empiric steroids pending workup.
- Screen for extra-articular SpA features (uveitis, IBD).

Concise, high-yield plan prioritizes imaging and serologic exclusion of malignancy before biologic therapy. (138 words)

Expert:

Hello, Expert Clinician here.

Best unifying diagnosis: Tumor-induced osteomalacia (FGF23-mediated hypophosphatemic osteomalacia) causing diffuse skeletal pain and profound proximal weakness. The pattern can mimic inflammatory back pain, but the major pivot is severe progressive weakness requiring a cane/binder plus pain spreading to sternum/shoulder/neck—suggesting metabolic bone disease with insufficiency fractures/pseudofractures, not isolated axial SpA.

Top differentials
1. Axial spondyloarthritis – fits morning stiffness, improvement with activity/NSAIDs, chest wall pain; but does not explain marked limb weakness well.
2. Multiple myeloma/metastatic cancer – fits nocturnal pain and smoking risk; less fitting for long inflammatory-type stiffness and diffuse enthesopathic pattern.
3. Tuberculous spondylitis – epidemiologic risk, chronic course; but multifocal noncontiguous pain and proximal weakness are less typical.

Most important next move: obtain CMP + calcium, phosphate, alkaline phosphatase, PTH, 25-OH vitamin D, creatinine, and spine/pelvis imaging. If low phosphate with high/normal ALP is found, check TmP/GFR and FGF23, then localize tumor with functional imaging (e.g., DOTATATE PET/CT).

Teaching pearl: In adults, bone pain + proximal muscle weakness + fragility symptoms = check phosphate; osteomalacia is commonly missed.

3. GPA/other vasculitis – could explain cavitary nodules and oral lesions, but not the ileal pathology/spondyloarthritis pattern.

Key pivot: the case was solved when “TB” failed treatment and tissue remained negative, while the bowel pathology and later anti-TNF response unified all organ systems.

Most important next move: continue GI-rheumatology co-management, monitor Crohn activity and biologic safety, and address smoking cessation.

Teaching pearl: Crohn can present first as inflammatory back pain; rare necrobiotic pulmonary nodules may mimic TB or vasculitis.
