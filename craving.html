Case Analysis & Key Findings

  1. Refeeding Syndrome (75%): This is the primary clinical suspicion. The shift from a catabolic to an anabolic state triggered by a sudden increase in glucose intake leads to insulin secretion. To refine the diagnosis and ensure patient safety, I recommend the following:

This autosomal recessive disorder results in defective thiazide-sensitive Na-Cl cotransporter function in the distal convoluted tubule, leading to hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, and metabolic alkalosis.

Key Clinical Insights

  1. Genetic Confirmation: The SLC12A3 mutation (c.2869A→T; p.Lys947Ter) confirms Gitelman Syndrome, explaining the lifelong electrolyte imbalances and salt-craving behaviors.
  2. Therapeutic Response: The normalization of potassium levels with amiloride, a potassium-sparing diuretic, underscores the renal tubular origin of the electrolyte disturbances.
  3. Clinical Mimicry: The case highlights how Gitelman Syndrome can mimic eating disorders due to overlapping symptoms like weight loss, malaise, and electrolyte imbalances.

Management and Follow-up

  1. Long-term Management: The patient requires ongoing electrolyte supplementation (magnesium, potassium, sodium chloride) and amiloride to maintain electrolyte balance.
  2. Dietary Recommendations: A high-salt diet is essential to compensate for renal sodium chloride wasting, which is counterintuitive in most other conditions.
  3. Genetic Counseling: Given the consanguinity and family history, genetic counseling is crucial for the patient and family to understand the hereditary nature of the condition.

Teaching Pearl

Gitelman Syndrome as a Diagnostic Challenge: This condition often presents with symptoms that overlap with eating disorders, particularly in adolescent females. Key historical clues such as lifelong salt-craving, nocturnal enuresis, and a family history of similar symptoms should prompt consideration of a genetic tubulopathy. The urinary calcium-to-creatinine ratio is a valuable tool in differentiating Gitelman (typically low) from Bartter syndrome (normal to high), although it may not always be definitive. Genetic testing provides the ultimate confirmation and guides appropriate management.
