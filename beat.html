Round 1

User: A 45-year-old man presented at a local hospital after a syncopal event while watching a baseball game. The event was preceded by sudden light-headedness, followed by several seconds of loss of consciousness without post-ictal confusion. He reported nausea and diaphoresis but denied chest pain. On-scene electrocardiogram (ECG) revealed monomorphic, wide-complex tachycardia at a rate of 240 beats per minute with a blood pressure of 110/50 mm Hg. Intravenous amiodarone was administered without effect. On arrival at the hospital, his systolic blood pressure dropped to 70 mm Hg, necessitating immediate electrical cardioversion, which successfully restored sinus rhythm. Pertinent ECG findings before cardioversion included QS complexes across the precordium (negative precordial concordance) and right-axis deviation, supporting a diagnosis of ventricular tachycardia.

Resident:

Hello, Chief Resident here.

Key findings include a syncopal event preceded by prodromal symptoms, followed by hemodynamically unstable monomorphic wide-complex tachycardia (WCT) at 240 bpm. The ECG features—negative precordial concordance and right-axis deviation—are highly specific for Ventricular Tachycardia (VT). The lack of response to amiodarone and subsequent hypotension necessitated emergent cardioversion.

Differential Diagnoses:
1. Ischemic Cardiomyopathy (Scar-mediated VT): 45% – Despite his age, a prior silent infarct or premature CAD remains the most common substrate for monomorphic VT.
2. Arrhythmogenic Right Ventricular Cardiomyopathy (ARVC): 25% – Suggested by the axis and potential RV origin; often presents with exercise-induced VT/syncope.
3. Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM): 15% – A leading cause of sudden cardiac arrest/syncope in middle-aged adults; VT is a common mechanism.
4. Idiopathic Outflow Tract VT: 10% – Usually benign, but high rates can cause hemodynamic collapse.
5. Sarcoidosis/Myocarditis: 5% – Inflammatory scars can serve as re-entrant circuits.

Recommended Next Steps:
1. High-sensitivity Troponin and Electrolytes: To assess for acute ischemia or metabolic triggers.
2. Transthoracic Echocardiogram: To evaluate for structural heart disease, EF, and wall motion abnormalities.
3. Cardiac MRI (with Gadolinium): To identify myocardial scarring, fibrofatty infiltration (ARVC), or edema.
4. Coronary Angiography: To rule out obstructive CAD as the underlying substrate.
5. Electrophysiology (EP) Study: For mapping and potential ablation once stabilized.

Critique & Improvements (5 points):
1. Amiodarone failure expected in scar VT; prioritize synchronized cardioversion earlier for instability.

5. Early EP consultation for risk stratification, ICD evaluation, and potential ablation.
6. Avoid over-reliance on single antiarrhythmic; prepare for lidocaine or procainamide if recurrent.

Stabilize, exclude reversible causes, and pursue secondary prevention.

Hello, Expert Clinician here.

Best unifying diagnosis:
Hemodynamically unstable sustained monomorphic ventricular tachycardia, most likely scar-mediated VT from structural heart disease until proven otherwise.

1. Ischemic scar VT – most common cause of monomorphic VT; fits reentrant pattern and malignant presentation, though age 45 is relatively young.

In an unstable wide-complex tachycardia, treat first as VT—don’t let diagnostic uncertainty delay cardioversion.
