[PHASE 1 — HISTORY PRESENTATION

]Q1 What is the physiological basis of orthopnea in a patient with Mitral Stenosis?
MODEL_ANS1 In the supine position, venous return to the right side of the heart increases, which shifts blood from the splanchnic and lower extremity beds to the central circulation. In Mitral Stenosis, the stenotic valve prevents adequate left atrial emptying, causing a rapid rise in pulmonary capillary wedge pressure, pulmonary congestion, and reduced lung compliance. This prompts the patient to sit up to relieve the dyspnea. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Cardiovascular System; Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part I, CVS).

Q2 How do you clinically differentiate Paroxysmal Nocturnal Dyspnea (PND) from orthopnea based on the patient's history?
MODEL_ANS2 Orthopnea occurs immediately or within minutes of lying flat and is rapidly relieved by sitting up or adding pillows. PND typically wakes the patient from sleep after 2 to 4 hours with acute shortness of breath and a choking sensation. It takes longer (usually 20-30 minutes) to resolve after the patient sits up or dangles their legs over the edge of the bed. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 1, CVS).

Q3 Your patient recently developed acute-onset palpitations with worsening dyspnea. Given her history of Mitral Stenosis, what is the most likely arrhythmia, and how does it precipitate failure?
MODEL_ANS3 Atrial Fibrillation (AF) is the most likely arrhythmia. Mitral Stenosis causes left atrial enlargement, predisposing to AF. The rapid ventricular rate in AF shortens the diastolic filling time. Since LV filling is already restricted by the stenotic valve, the shortened diastole drastically reduces cardiac output and acutely raises left atrial pressure, precipitating acute pulmonary edema. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, CVS).

Q4 The patient has a history of an ischemic CVA 3 months ago. What is the pathophysiological link between her primary cardiac diagnosis and the cerebrovascular event?
MODEL_ANS4 Mitral Stenosis, particularly when complicated by Atrial Fibrillation or severe left atrial dilatation, leads to blood stasis in the left atrium and its appendage. This stasis strongly predisposes to thrombus formation. Embolization of a fragment of this thrombus to the cerebral circulation results in an ischemic stroke. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 1, CVS).

Q5 What are the different causes of hemoptysis in Mitral Stenosis, and which one is the most common?
MODEL_ANS5 Causes include: 1) Blood-streaked sputum due to chronic bronchitis ("winter bronchitis"), which is the most common. 2) Frank hemoptysis (pulmonary apoplexy) due to the rupture of dilated bronchial veins under high pressure. 3) Pink frothy sputum due to acute pulmonary edema. 4) Hemoptysis due to pulmonary infarction. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, CVS).

Q6 You accurately noted that she is NYHA Class III. What clinical criteria define New York Heart Association (NYHA) Class III heart failure?
MODEL_ANS6 NYHA Class III is defined as a marked limitation of physical activity. The patient is comfortable at rest, but less than ordinary physical activity (such as walking short distances or dressing) causes fatigue, palpitations, or dyspnea. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, CVS).

Q7 The patient is on 'Dytor Plus'. What are its pharmacological components, and why is this combination beneficial in congestive heart failure?
MODEL_ANS7 Dytor Plus contains Torsemide (a loop diuretic) and Spironolactone (an aldosterone antagonist/potassium-sparing diuretic). Torsemide relieves fluid overload and pulmonary congestion, while Spironolactone prevents loop diuretic-induced hypokalemia and provides mortality benefits in heart failure by inhibiting aldosterone-induced myocardial fibrosis. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 1).

Q8 The patient has a smoking history of "20 bidis per day for 30 years". How do you calculate her smoking index and pack-years, and what is its significance?
MODEL_ANS8 Smoking Index = (number of bidis smoked per day) × (number of years smoked) = 20 × 30 = 600. An index >300 indicates a heavy smoker. Pack-years = (Bidis per day / 20) × years smoked = (20/20) × 30 = 30 pack-years. Heavy smoking increases the risk of COPD, coronary artery disease, and cor pulmonale. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 1, Respiratory System).

Q9 You mentioned a negative history of recurrent sore throat. Why is this specific negative history crucial in this patient?
MODEL_ANS9 A history of recurrent sore throat, particularly in childhood or adolescence, points towards Group A Beta-Hemolytic Streptococcal pharyngitis. This is the initiating infection that triggers Acute Rheumatic Fever, eventually leading to chronic Rheumatic Heart Disease and Mitral Stenosis. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, CVS).

Q10 What is the significance of asking about hoarseness of voice in a patient with severe Mitral Stenosis?
MODEL_ANS10 Hoarseness of voice in Mitral Stenosis can be due to Ortner's syndrome, a condition where a massively dilated left atrium or an enlarged pulmonary artery compresses the left recurrent laryngeal nerve against the aortic arch. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 1, CVS).

Q11 The patient presents with a cough. What differentiates a "cardiac cough" from a respiratory cough in the history?
MODEL_ANS11 A cardiac cough is typically due to pulmonary venous congestion causing bronchial mucosal edema. It is often dry or produces thin whitish sputum (unlike the purulent sputum of respiratory infections), worsens at night (often preceding or accompanying PND), and is aggravated by exertion or lying flat. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, CVS).

Q12 She is taking Carvedilol. What is its mechanism of action, and why must it be prescribed with caution in this specific patient?
MODEL_ANS12 Carvedilol is a non-selective beta-blocker with alpha-1 blocking properties. Because she has a 30 pack-year smoking history, she may have underlying COPD. Non-selective beta-blockers can precipitate severe bronchospasm in such patients and must be used with extreme caution. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 1).

Q13 You documented that she is taking Thyroxine. How can thyroid dysfunction directly impact her current cardiac status?
MODEL_ANS13 Over-replacement of thyroxine (iatrogenic hyperthyroidism) can induce a high-output state, tachycardia, and precipitate or worsen Atrial Fibrillation, exacerbating her heart failure. Conversely, under-treated hypothyroidism can cause bradycardia, diastolic dysfunction, and pericardial effusion. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Endocrine System).

Q14 What negative history should you routinely seek to clinically rule out the progression to Right Heart Failure?
MODEL_ANS14 To rule out Right Heart Failure, one must ask for a history of swelling of the feet/ankles (pedal edema), abdominal distension (ascites), early satiety, and right upper quadrant abdominal pain (due to hepatic congestion stretching Glisson's capsule). (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 1, CVS).

Q15 When the patient mentions palpitations, you ruled out syncope and presyncope. Why is this hemodynamically significant in the context of Mitral Stenosis?
MODEL_ANS15 In Mitral Stenosis, palpitations often represent new-onset or poorly controlled Atrial Fibrillation. Ruling out syncope, presyncope, and dizziness helps confirm that despite the rapid ventricular response, the patient is currently maintaining adequate cerebral perfusion and cardiac output, indicating she is not in acute hemodynamic collapse or shock. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, CVS).

]Q1 In this case, what is your diagnosis from physical examination alone?

MODEL_ANS1 From examination alone, you have evidence of tachypnoea with bilateral infrascapular fine crepitations, suggesting pulmonary congestion, but you have not demonstrated the classical auscultatory signs of mitral stenosis. A complete bedside diagnosis should state whether there is evidence of MS, pulmonary hypertension, CCF, and rhythm abnormality. Kundu’s standard valvular diagnosis format includes anatomical lesion, structural abnormality, aetiology, complications, and rhythm.

Q2 What are the classical peripheral and precordial signs of mitral stenosis?

MODEL_ANS2 Classical signs include low-volume pulse, normal or low BP, tapping apex, diastolic thrill at mitral area, loud S1, opening snap, and a low-pitched mid-diastolic rumbling murmur with presystolic accentuation best heard at the apex with the bell in left lateral position during expiration. Alagappan also lists left parasternal heave, palpable P2, diastolic thrill, loud S1, opening snap and mid-diastolic murmur as signs of MS.

Q3 Your pulse is 64/min regular, but the patient had palpitations. What bedside check is mandatory?

MODEL_ANS3 You must count the apical heart rate and radial pulse simultaneously for one full minute to detect pulse deficit, especially because atrial fibrillation is common in MS. Alagappan defines pulse deficit as the difference between heart rate and pulse rate and lists atrial fibrillation as a cause; Kundu notes that pulse becomes irregular in MS when atrial fibrillation develops.

Q4 How should JVP have been reported?

MODEL_ANS4 “JVP normal” is incomplete. You should state the patient’s position, vertical height above the sternal angle, waveform if visible, hepatojugular reflux, and whether prominent a-wave or v-wave is present. In pulmonary hypertension, Kundu notes prominent a-wave, and in functional tricuspid incompetence prominent v-wave may occur.

Q5 What is the significance of bilateral basal fine crepitations in this patient?

MODEL_ANS5 In a patient with MS and orthopnoea/PND, bilateral fine basal crepitations suggest left atrial failure/pulmonary oedema or pulmonary congestion. Kundu states that after left atrial failure in MS, S1 may become muffled, opening snap may be absent, presystolic murmur may disappear, and bilateral fine basal crepitations may appear.

Q6 If no murmur is heard, can mitral stenosis still be present?

MODEL_ANS6 Yes. A murmur may be missed if auscultation is not done correctly, or it may be soft/absent in severe low-output states or during left atrial failure. Alagappan notes that the MS murmur may disappear in severe MS due to decreased cardiac output; Kundu notes that in left atrial failure S1 may become muffled, opening snap not heard, and presystolic component disappears.

Q7 How should you auscultate specifically for the murmur of MS?

MODEL_ANS7 Use the bell of the stethoscope lightly at the apex/mitral area, put the patient in left lateral position, ask for expiration, and repeat after mild exercise if the condition permits. The murmur is classically low-pitched, rumbling, mid-diastolic, often with presystolic accentuation.

Q8 What is a tapping apex beat and why is it important in MS?

MODEL_ANS8 A tapping apex beat is a palpable, accentuated S1 at the apex. It is an important bedside clue to MS. Kundu states that the apex in MS is normally positioned and tapping due to accentuated S1; Alagappan also lists tapping apical impulse as a sign of MS.

Q9 What signs would suggest pulmonary hypertension in this patient?

MODEL_ANS9 Signs include low-volume pulse, prominent a-wave in JVP, central cyanosis if advanced, visible pulmonary artery pulsation, epigastric pulsation, palpable P2, left parasternal heave, loud P2, pulmonary ejection systolic murmur, Graham Steell murmur, and right-sided S3. Kundu emphasizes epigastric pulsation, left parasternal heave, and outward apex as RVH features in pulmonary hypertension.

Q10 How do you palpate for left parasternal heave?

MODEL_ANS10 Place the heel/ulnar border of the right hand vertically over the lower left parasternal area, especially the 3rd to 5th intercostal spaces, with the patient supine; look and feel for a sustained lifting impulse. Kundu states that left parasternal heave is most commonly due to RVH.

Q11 What is the expected apex position in uncomplicated MS?

MODEL_ANS11 In uncomplicated MS, the apex is usually in the left 5th intercostal space just medial to the midclavicular line and is tapping in character. In established MS with pulmonary hypertension, the impulse may become diffuse and RV-type with parasternal heave.

Q12 What should be examined in other systems in a CVS long case?

MODEL_ANS12 Respiratory examination should look for tachypnoea, orthopnoea, hydrothorax, basal crepitations and rhonchi; abdominal examination should look for tender hepatomegaly, pulsatile liver, splenomegaly and ascites; neurological examination is relevant because hemiplegia/monoplegia may occur in MS, especially with atrial fibrillation.

Q13 How do you differentiate true MS murmur from an Austin Flint murmur?

MODEL_ANS13 True MS usually has loud S1, opening snap, diastolic thrill and presystolic accentuation. Austin Flint murmur occurs in severe AR, has no opening snap, no loud S1, no diastolic thrill, and is associated with peripheral signs of AR and an AR murmur. Kundu lists these differentiating points.

Q14 What is the significance of opening snap in MS?

MODEL_ANS14 Opening snap indicates organic MS with pliable, non-calcified valve cusps, significant LA-LV gradient and suitability for intervention; a shorter A2–OS interval suggests more severe stenosis. It may be absent in calcific MS, severe MS with low output, AF, left atrial failure, pulmonary hypertension, or associated lesions.

Q15 What bedside evidence supports decompensation in this patient?

MODEL_ANS15 Tachypnoea at 24/min and bilateral infrascapular fine crepitations support pulmonary congestion. In the context of history of orthopnoea and PND, this suggests left-sided decompensation. Kundu describes PND/acute pulmonary oedema examination as severe dyspnoea, tachypnoea, raised JVP in attacks, S3 or underlying heart disease, and predominantly basal crepitations.

]Q1 You recorded a regular pulse rate of 64/min. In the history, the patient had acute-onset palpitations, raising the suspicion of Atrial Fibrillation. How do you explain the regular, normal-rate pulse observed today?
MODEL_ANS1 The patient could have had Paroxysmal Atrial Fibrillation that spontaneously reverted to normal sinus rhythm, or her palpitations might have been due to sinus tachycardia or premature beats. Additionally, she is taking Carvedilol (a beta-blocker), which suppresses the heart rate and can mask or control the ventricular response if she were in AF, though a completely regular rhythm suggests she is currently in sinus rhythm. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, CVS; Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part I).

Q2 You mentioned the JVP is "normal." How exactly do you measure the JVP clinically, and what is the normal upper limit?
MODEL_ANS2 The patient is positioned at a 45-degree angle with the neck slightly rotated. The vertical distance between the sternal angle of Louis and the upper limit of the venous pulsation in the internal jugular vein is measured using two scales. The normal limit is up to 3 to 4 cm above the sternal angle. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 1, General Examination).

Q3 Your palpation reveals the apical impulse at the 5th ICS, 1 cm medial to the mid-clavicular line. What specific character of the apical impulse is pathognomonic for pure Mitral Stenosis, and what is its physiological basis?
MODEL_ANS3 A "tapping" apical impulse is characteristic of pure Mitral Stenosis. It is not a true ventricular thrust; rather, it is the palpable equivalent of a loud first heart sound (S1), caused by the sudden, forceful closure of the thickened but pliable mitral valve leaflets. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, CVS).

Q4 Your notes mention "Parasternal Heaving" without specifying presence or absence. If a parasternal heave were present, what does it signify in this patient, and which part of your hand do you use to feel it?
MODEL_ANS4 A parasternal heave signifies Right Ventricular Hypertrophy (RVH), which in this patient would be secondary to pulmonary hypertension caused by her Mitral Stenosis. It is best appreciated by placing the ulnar border of the right hand over the left parasternal area. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 1, CVS).

Q5 You checked for a precordial thrill. To specifically detect the diastolic thrill of Mitral Stenosis, what specific positioning and breathing maneuvers should you ask the patient to perform?
MODEL_ANS5 The diastolic thrill of Mitral Stenosis is a low-frequency vibration. It is best felt at the apex with the flat of the palm or the palmar aspect of the metacarpophalangeal joints, with the patient in the left lateral decubitus position, during held end-expiration. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 1, CVS).

Q6 You documented "S1 and S2 heard". How is the first heart sound (S1) characteristically altered in early to moderate Mitral Stenosis, and why?
MODEL_ANS6 S1 is characteristically "loud" or accentuated. Because of the elevated left atrial pressure, the mitral valve leaflets are kept wide open during diastole and only close when ventricular systole begins. This wide excursion and the tensing of thickened leaflets produce a loud sound. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, CVS).

Q7 In the context of her worsening dyspnea, if she has developed severe pulmonary arterial hypertension, how would the second heart sound (S2) change on auscultation?
MODEL_ANS7 The pulmonary component of the second heart sound (P2) becomes loud and palpable (palpable P2). The S2 may also become narrowly split because the hypertrophied right ventricle empties earlier against the high pulmonary pressure. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 1, CVS).

Q8 Aside from S1 and S2, what specific extra heart sound should you actively listen for in early diastole in a patient with Rheumatic Mitral Stenosis?
MODEL_ANS8 An Opening Snap (OS). It is a high-pitched, early diastolic sound heard just after S2, caused by the sudden tensing of the fused mitral valve commissures as the leaflets dome into the left ventricle during early diastole. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, CVS).

Q9 You reported "no murmur" in a known case of Mitral Stenosis. What is this clinical phenomenon called, and what are the potential causes for it in this patient?
MODEL_ANS9 This is called "Silent Mitral Stenosis." It occurs when the flow across the stenotic valve is severely reduced (e.g., severe right heart failure, severe pulmonary hypertension), if the valve is heavily calcified and immobile, or due to patient factors like obesity (her BMI is 27) and emphysema (she has a 30-pack-year smoking history). (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 1, CVS).

Q10 If you suspect "Silent Mitral Stenosis", what specific maneuvers can you employ to try and unmask the characteristic localized mid-diastolic murmur?
MODEL_ANS10 You should auscultate using the bell of the stethoscope placed lightly at the apex, with the patient rolled into the left lateral position. You should listen during expiration and can also ask the patient to do mild exercise (like sitting up and lying down quickly, or coughing) to increase heart rate and flow across the valve. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 1, CVS).

Q11 You noted bilateral fine crepitations in the infrascapular region. Given the absence of fever and purulent sputum, what is the most likely pathophysiological mechanism for these findings?
MODEL_ANS11 The fine crepitations represent pulmonary congestion or early pulmonary edema. The stenotic mitral valve causes backpressure, leading to elevated left atrial and pulmonary venous pressures. When pulmonary capillary hydrostatic pressure exceeds plasma oncotic pressure, transudation of fluid into the alveoli occurs. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, CVS).

Q12 The patient's BMI is 27 kg/m². How does this anthropometric finding correlate with your examination of the precordium?
MODEL_ANS12 A BMI of 27 classifies her as overweight. Increased chest wall adiposity can dampen the transmission of cardiac impulses and acoustic vibrations, which can make the apical impulse impalpable and heart sounds or murmurs faint. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 1, General Examination).

Q13 You mentioned the thyroid was not visualized. As she has a history of hypothyroidism, how do you properly palpate the thyroid gland to check for goiter or nodules?
MODEL_ANS13 The examiner should stand behind the patient, ask them to slightly flex their neck (to relax the sternocleidomastoid muscles), place the fingers of both hands on either side of the trachea just below the cricoid cartilage, and palpate while asking the patient to swallow a sip of water. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 1, General Examination).

Q14 You did not present an abdominal examination. In a patient with Mitral Stenosis and previous Congestive Cardiac Failure, what critical signs of Right Heart Failure must you look for in the abdomen?
MODEL_ANS14 You must examine the abdomen for tender hepatomegaly (due to hepatic venous congestion), hepatojugular reflux, and signs of ascites (such as shifting dullness), which would indicate progression to Right Heart Failure. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Abdomen/CVS).

Q15 Is a third heart sound (S3) typically present in pure Mitral Stenosis? Why or why not?
MODEL_ANS15 S3 is characteristically absent in pure, severe Mitral Stenosis. An S3 is generated by the rapid rushing of blood into a compliant ventricle during early diastole. In MS, the stenotic valve obstructs this rapid filling phase. If an S3 is heard, it strongly suggests concomitant Mitral Regurgitation or another pathology. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 1, CVS).
