MODEL_ANS1 Document the maximum temperature, site and method of measurement, duration of each episode, frequency, diurnal variation, whether temperature returns to normal between episodes, sweating, response to antipyretics or antibiotics, and associated symptoms. “Evening rise” and “intermittent fever” should not be accepted without a documented temperature chart. Chills are a sensation of cold, whereas rigor implies marked shivering, teeth-chattering and piloerection.

Q2 Which additional history would you seek to assess for infective endocarditis?

MODEL_ANS2 Ask about recent dental extraction or dental infection, skin or soft-tissue infection, intravenous cannulation, urinary catheterisation, cystoscopy, gastrointestinal procedures, previous valve disease, prosthetic valves, intravenous drug use and antibiotic exposure. Also ask about new focal neurological symptoms, haematuria, loin pain, painful digits, skin lesions and splenic pain as possible embolic or immunological manifestations.

Q3 How should the severity of his dyspnoea be graded?

MODEL_ANS3 Determine the exact activity producing breathlessness and assign an NYHA functional class. Symptoms with less-than-ordinary activity and marked limitation correspond to NYHA class III; symptoms at rest or with any activity correspond to class IV. “Routine activity” and “minimal exertion” should be replaced by examples such as walking distance, bathing, dressing or climbing stairs.

Q4 What further questions are required to establish orthopnoea?

MODEL_ANS4 Ask whether breathlessness begins within minutes of lying flat, how many pillows are required, whether the patient sleeps sitting upright, and whether symptoms improve immediately on sitting or standing. Orthopnoea is recumbent dyspnoea relieved by sitting up or elevating the head.

Q5 How would you establish that the nocturnal attacks are truly paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea?

MODEL_ANS5 Confirm that the patient falls asleep comfortably, awakens after approximately 2–5 hours with severe breathlessness or choking, and obtains relief after sitting or standing for several minutes. Ask about associated cough, wheeze, sweating and pink frothy sputum. PND is a manifestation of decompensation of the left heart and must be distinguished from nocturnal asthma, obstructive sleep apnoea and panic attacks.

Q6 Which respiratory symptoms must still be sought despite the negative history of cough?

MODEL_ANS6 Enquire specifically about wheeze, sputum, haemoptysis, pleuritic pain, previous asthma or COPD, smoking and passive-smoking exposure. In dyspnoea, the history should document onset, progression, exertional threshold, posture, seasonal variation, associated wheeze or cough and response to rest, posture, inhalers or other treatment.

Q7 How should the palpitations be characterized more precisely?

MODEL_ANS7 Ask about duration and frequency of each episode, sudden or gradual onset and termination, perceived regularity, rapidity, skipped beats, neck pulsations and whether symptoms occur at rest or during exertion. Associated chest discomfort, presyncope, syncope, sweating and dyspnoea must be documented. Palpitation is an unpleasant awareness of forceful, rapid or arrhythmic cardiac beating and may occur with extrasystoles, tachyarrhythmias, pyrexia, anaemia, drugs or anxiety.

Q8 What further details are necessary regarding the lower-limb oedema?

MODEL_ANS8 Establish its time of first appearance, diurnal variation, symmetry, upper level, associated pain or redness, sacral or facial oedema, abdominal distension, rapid weight gain and response to elevation or diuretics. Cardiac oedema typically begins in dependent areas around the ankles and pretibial regions and ascends progressively; sacral oedema may predominate in a bed-bound patient.

Q9 Which features would help distinguish cardiac oedema from renal or hepatic oedema?

MODEL_ANS9 Ask about periorbital puffiness, frothy urine, reduced urine output, haematuria, previous renal disease, jaundice and whether abdominal distension preceded pedal swelling. Cardiac oedema usually progresses from legs upward and accompanies dyspnoea; nephrotic oedema commonly begins around the face in the morning, while hepatic oedema is generally preceded by ascites.

Q10 Which precipitating factors for acute decompensated heart failure should be sought?

MODEL_ANS10 Ask about discontinuation of cardiac or antihypertensive medicines, excess salt or fluid intake, recent infection, fever preceding breathlessness, new arrhythmia, myocardial ischaemia, anaemia and nephrotoxic or fluid-retaining drugs. The temporal relationship among fever, palpitations, dyspnoea and oedema should be reconstructed carefully rather than merely listing equal durations.

MODEL_ANS11 Ask about pressure-like chest discomfort, radiation, exertional relation, diaphoresis, nausea and relief with rest or nitrates. Therefore, episodes reported as lasting 24–48 hours cannot automatically be labelled TIAs.

]Q1 You mentioned the patient has a history of multiple episodes of right-sided weakness improving within 24-48 hours. What is the clinical terminology for this, and what is its most likely etiology in this patient?
MODEL_ANS1 These episodes represent Transient Ischemic Attacks (TIAs) or Reversible Ischemic Neurologic Deficits (RINDs). Given the patient's history of recurrent MIs, the most likely etiology is cardioembolic (e.g., a mural thrombus or atrial fibrillation) or severe atherosclerotic carotid disease secondary to his uncontrolled hypertension and diabetes. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part I, Nervous System).

Q2 The patient experienced sudden, painless, gradual loss of vision in 2019-2020. What is the most common vascular cause of this symptom in a patient with a high cardiovascular risk profile?
MODEL_ANS2 Sudden painless loss of vision is classically caused by Central Retinal Artery Occlusion (CRAO), often due to microemboli (amaurosis fugax) originating from the carotid arteries or the heart. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Nervous System).

Q3 How do you pathophysiologically explain Paroxysmal Nocturnal Dyspnea (PND) in this patient?
MODEL_ANS3 PND occurs in left ventricular failure. When the patient lies flat, dependent edema from the lower limbs is reabsorbed into the central circulation, increasing venous return (preload). The failing left ventricle cannot pump this extra volume, leading to increased pulmonary venous pressure and acute pulmonary congestion, which awakens the patient 2-4 hours after falling asleep. (Reference: Kundu, Part I, Cardiovascular System).

Q4 The patient has a history of fever with chills and rigors for 15 days alongside new-onset heart failure symptoms and palpitations. What life-threatening cardiovascular infection must be ruled out?
MODEL_ANS4 Infective Endocarditis (IE). A new-onset fever with chills in a patient with underlying structural heart disease (prior MIs, potential mural thrombus, or valvular dysfunction) and new arrhythmias or heart failure symptoms is a classic presentation for IE. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Cardiovascular System).

Q5 Why might this patient not have presented with classic chest pain during his myocardial infarctions or current cardiac decompensation?
MODEL_ANS5 The patient is a known case of Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus since 2013. Diabetic autonomic neuropathy can blunt the afferent pain fibers from the heart, leading to "silent ischemia" or painless myocardial infarctions. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Endocrine and Metabolic Diseases).

Q6 You noted that the bilateral lower-limb swelling progressed from the ankles to the thighs. What mechanism explains pitting edema in this clinical scenario?
MODEL_ANS6 Pitting edema here is likely due to right ventricular failure (secondary to left ventricular failure, resulting in biventricular failure). The failing right heart causes an increase in systemic venous pressure and capillary hydrostatic pressure, forcing fluid into the interstitial space. (Reference: Kundu, Part I, General Examination).

Q7 What key characteristic of the palpitations should you have asked about to help differentiate its etiology?
MODEL_ANS7 You should have asked whether the palpitations were regular or irregular, fast or slow, and if they were associated with "skipped beats" or syncope. Irregularly irregular palpitations would strongly suggest Atrial Fibrillation, which fits perfectly with his embolic stroke history. (Reference: Kundu, Part I, Cardiovascular System).

Q8 An evening rise in temperature is noted in the history. While classically associated with Tuberculosis, what other chronic conditions must be considered in this elderly man?
MODEL_ANS8 While Tuberculosis is the classic cause in India, an evening rise in temperature can also occur in chronic suppurative infections, lymphomas, connective tissue disorders, or subacute infective endocarditis. (Reference: Kundu, Part I, General Examination).

Q9 What is the significance of the patient taking an SSRI and an atypical antipsychotic, considering his current presentation of palpitations?
MODEL_ANS9 Both SSRIs and atypical antipsychotics are known to cause QT interval prolongation. In a patient with ischemic heart disease, this increases the risk of ventricular arrhythmias (e.g., Torsades de Pointes), which could present as sudden episodic palpitations and shortness of breath. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Cardiovascular System).

Q10 According to the New York Heart Association (NYHA) classification, what class of dyspnea is this patient currently in?
MODEL_ANS10 The patient has dyspnea on minimal exertion and Paroxysmal Nocturnal Dyspnea, which places him in NYHA Class III (marked limitation of physical activity, comfortable at rest, but less than ordinary activity causes symptoms) to Class IV (inability to carry on any physical activity without discomfort). (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Cardiovascular System).

Q11 The patient has a history of renal calculi (operated in 2003) and current edema. Why is it important to ask about facial puffiness or frothy urine?
MODEL_ANS11 To rule out a renal cause for the edema (such as diabetic nephropathy). Renal edema typically presents first in areas with loose connective tissue, like the periorbital region, and is worse in the morning, whereas cardiac edema is dependent and worsens by evening. (Reference: Kundu, Part I, General Examination).

Q12 How does orthopnea fundamentally differ from PND in terms of onset time?
MODEL_ANS12 Orthopnea occurs almost immediately (within a few minutes) upon lying down and is relieved promptly by sitting up. PND occurs after 2-4 hours of sleep and takes longer (15-30 minutes) to resolve after sitting or standing. (Reference: Kundu, Part I, Cardiovascular System).

Q13 Based on the recurrent focal neurological deficits and cardiovascular risk factors, what kind of dementia might this patient be at risk of developing (or already have)?
MODEL_ANS13 Vascular dementia or Multi-infarct dementia. The history of multiple strokes, quadriparesis, and aphasia in a hypertensive, diabetic patient is highly characteristic of stepwise cognitive decline seen in vascular dementia. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Nervous System).

Q14 What pertinent negative history regarding the GI system is crucial when evaluating a patient with right heart failure symptoms?
MODEL_ANS14 Right heart failure leads to systemic venous congestion, causing hepatomegaly and gut edema. Crucial negative history includes right upper quadrant pain (stretching of the liver capsule), early satiety, nausea, and anorexia. (Reference: Kundu, Part I, Cardiovascular System).

Q15 What does the term "irregular medication" imply regarding target organ damage in this specific case?
MODEL_ANS15 Poor adherence to anti-hypertensive and anti-diabetic medications accelerates macrovascular complications (atherosclerosis leading to MIs and strokes) and microvascular complications (retinopathy, neuropathy). This non-compliance is the direct driver of his extensive multimorbidity. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Diabetes Mellitus).

Q2 Is the pulse examination complete?

MODEL_ANS2 No. In addition to rate, rhythm, character and arterial delays, document volume, vessel-wall condition, peripheral pulses, pulse deficit and special characters such as pulsus alternans. A regular pulse of 76/min at one examination does not exclude the episodic tachyarrhythmia described in the history. Kundu lists pulsus alternans among the cardinal bedside signs of left ventricular failure.

Q3 Which cardiovascular findings must be examined in view of the presenting complaints?

MODEL_ANS3 Measure the JVP and hepatojugular reflux; inspect and palpate the precordium; locate and characterize the apex beat; look for parasternal heave and thrills; and auscultate S1, S2, S3, S4, murmurs and pericardial rub. Examine for basal crepitations, peripheral oedema, congestive hepatomegaly and ascites. Raised JVP, peripheral oedema and hepatomegaly suggest right-sided failure, while basal crepitations, gallop rhythm, pulsus alternans and pulmonary oedema support left-sided failure.

Q4 Why is a complete respiratory examination mandatory?

MODEL_ANS4 The patient presented with progressive dyspnoea, orthopnoea and PND. Respiratory examination should include chest shape and symmetry, tracheal position, chest expansion, percussion note, breath sounds, added sounds—especially basal fine crepitations—and evidence of pleural effusion. Alagappan describes pulmonary crackles, pulmonary oedema and pleural effusion among manifestations of cardiac congestion.

Q5 How should the MMSE score of 24/30 be interpreted?

MODEL_ANS5 It demonstrates impaired performance, predominantly in delayed recall and the unfinished or abnormal language–visuospatial items, but it should not be used alone to establish a specific dementia. Kundu describes MMSE as a 30-point bedside assessment of orientation, registration, attention, recall and language, and identifies scores below 21 as associated with cognitive impairment. Here, poor vision may also have interfered with reading, writing and copying, so “not completed” must be separated from “incorrect.”

Q6 What is the localization suggested by finger agnosia?

MODEL_ANS6 Finger agnosia is a cortical sign associated with dominant parietal or parieto-occipital dysfunction, particularly when accompanied by acalculia, agraphia and right–left disorientation. These accompanying components should therefore be tested before claiming a complete Gerstmann-type syndrome.

Q7 What is the significance of dressing apraxia, and how do you reconcile it with finger agnosia?

MODEL_ANS7 Dressing and constructional apraxia are classically associated with nondominant parietal dysfunction, whereas finger agnosia more commonly points toward the dominant parietal region. Their coexistence may indicate bilateral or multifocal cortical disease, but both findings must first be demonstrated reliably, especially because visual impairment and poor comprehension can produce false-positive results.

Q8 Why is the optic-nerve examination presently inadequate?

MODEL_ANS8 Visual acuity must be recorded separately for each eye using an exact measure; visual fields must specify the defect; colour vision must be quantified; and fundoscopy must document the optic discs, retina and vessels. Kundu lists visual acuity, visual fields, colour vision and fundoscopy as the four essential components of optic-nerve examination.

Q9 How would you confirm a left sixth-nerve palsy?

MODEL_ANS9 Demonstrate impaired abduction of the left eye in the cardinal positions of gaze and ask about horizontal diplopia, maximal when looking toward the left and at distance. Record the primary position of the eyes and exclude conjugate-gaze palsy or mechanical restriction. The sixth nerve supplies the lateral rectus and, because of its long intracranial course, may also be affected as a false-localizing sign in raised intracranial pressure.

Q10 How should the possible ptosis and anisocoria be documented?

MODEL_ANS10 Record the side and degree of ptosis, palpebral-fissure width, pupil diameters in bright and dim illumination, pupil shape, direct and consensual responses, accommodation and ocular alignment. “Possible anisocoria with ptosis” is not adequate because third-nerve palsy, Horner syndrome and local ocular causes produce different combinations of pupil and eyelid abnormalities.

Q11 How do rigidity and spasticity differ clinically?

MODEL_ANS11 Rigidity is extrapyramidal hypertonia affecting agonists and antagonists throughout the range of passive movement; it may be lead-pipe or cogwheel. Spasticity is pyramidal, velocity-dependent and usually distributed preferentially among antigravity muscles. Cogwheel rigidity is best appreciated at the wrist and neck, while lead-pipe rigidity is commonly evident in the legs and trunk.

Q12 Are bilateral weakness and a unilateral extensor plantar response typical of uncomplicated idiopathic parkinsonism?

MODEL_ANS12 No. In uncomplicated parkinsonism, objective power is generally preserved, deep reflexes are normal, clonus is absent and plantar responses are flexor. Reproducible left extensor plantar response indicates additional corticospinal involvement and should not be attributed to rigidity. This is an atypical or additional neurological sign.

Q13 Is the description of a stepping gait internally consistent?

Since ankle power was recorded as 4/5 and no foot drop was documented, the gait must be re-examined. Parkinsonian gait would instead show short steps, reduced arm swing, difficulty initiating, festination and impaired turning; cerebellar gait is broad-based, irregular and staggering.

Q14 How should the cerebellar findings be verified and localized?

MODEL_ANS14 Record finger–nose and heel–shin tests, dysmetria, dysdiadochokinesia, decomposition, rebound, tandem gait and the side of abnormality. Limb weakness can itself produce clumsy movement and confound coordination testing. Cerebellar hemisphere lesions cause ipsilateral limb signs, vermian lesions cause truncal and gait ataxia, and dysdiadochokinesia is inability to perform rapid alternating movements smoothly.

Q15 What neurological syndrome can be stated from the examination without prematurely assigning an aetiology?

MODEL_ANS15 The examination suggests a multisystem neurological syndrome combining extrapyramidal rigidity, a left pyramidal sign, possible cerebellar dysfunction, cognitive or cortical abnormalities, visual dysfunction and possible oculomotor involvement. This is not a clean pattern of uncomplicated idiopathic Parkinson disease. Alagappan and Kundu describe parkinsonism-plus syndromes as parkinsonian features accompanied by cerebellar, oculomotor, cortical or autonomic dysfunction; however, multifocal vascular disease and unreliable or incomplete findings must be excluded before naming a specific disorder.

]Q1 You mentioned a MMSE score of 24/30 with a specific recall score of 0/3. In a patient with a history of multiple strokes, diabetes, and hypertension, what does this disproportionate loss of recall suggest, and how do you differentiate it clinically from Alzheimer's dementia?

Unlike Alzheimer's disease, which typically presents with an insidious and smooth progression of memory loss, vascular dementia is characterized by a step-wise deterioration, focal neurological deficits (like his previous hemiparesis and current extensor plantar), and a high burden of cardiovascular risk factors. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Nervous System - Higher Functions).

Q2 You noted the presence of both finger agnosia and dressing apraxia. Which specific cerebral lobes are responsible for these two distinct findings?
MODEL_ANS2 Finger agnosia is classically a feature of Gerstmann syndrome, which localizes to a lesion in the dominant (usually left) parietal lobe (angular gyrus). Dressing apraxia, on the other hand, typically localizes to a lesion in the non-dominant (usually right) parietal lobe. Finding both suggests bilateral cortical or subcortical involvement, consistent with a multi-infarct state. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Nervous System - Visuospatial Functions).

Q3 The patient has left lateral rectus weakness (CN VI palsy). In a diabetic and hypertensive patient with no signs of raised intracranial pressure, what is the most likely etiology of this isolated cranial nerve finding?
MODEL_ANS3 In an elderly patient with diabetes and hypertension, an isolated 6th cranial nerve palsy is most often due to ischemic microvascular mononeuropathy (vasa nervorum ischemia). It is also known as a "false localizing sign" if associated with raised intracranial pressure, but ischemia is the leading cause here. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part I, Nervous System - Cranial Nerves).

Q4 You noted "anisocoria with ptosis" but were unclear on the side. If the patient had a left-sided partial ptosis and a left pupil that was smaller than the right (miosis), what syndrome does this represent, and what pharmacological test confirms it?
MODEL_ANS4 This represents Horner's Syndrome (oculosympathetic paresis), characterized by unilateral partial ptosis, miosis, and anhidrosis. It can be confirmed by instilling a topical drop of 4% cocaine, which will dilate a normal pupil but fail to dilate the Horner's pupil, or 1% apraclonidine, which reverses the anisocoria. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Nervous System).

Q5 Cogwheel rigidity was present in both upper limbs. What is the pathophysiological mechanism of cogwheel rigidity, and how does it differ from clasp-knife spasticity?
MODEL_ANS5 Cogwheel rigidity is a type of extrapyramidal hypertonia where a superimposed tremor creates a "catch and release" sensation on passive movement of a joint. Clasp-knife spasticity, conversely, is a pyramidal (UMN) sign characterized by initial resistance to passive stretch that suddenly gives way. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part I, Nervous System - Motor System).

Q6 You recorded the plantar response as "Left: extensor". What is the clinical eponym for this sign, and what anatomical tract is damaged to produce it?
MODEL_ANS6 This is Babinski's sign. An extensor plantar response indicates structural damage to the pyramidal tract (upper motor neuron lesion) on the contralateral side. In this case, a left extensor plantar indicates a right-sided UMN lesion. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part I, Nervous System - Reflexes).
