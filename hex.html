USER (Case History):

Ms. V.P., 21-year-old unmarried female, resident of Ghaziabad

Chief Complaints:
  • Abdominal pain for 3 months
  • Fever for 3 months
  • Excessive menstrual bleeding / polymenometrorrhagia for 3 months
  • Generalized swelling for 2 months
  • Generalized weakness for 2 months
History of Present Illness:
The patient was asymptomatic around 3 months back, when she developed abdominal pain.
Abdominal Pain: Insidious onset, intermittent, colicky, located in epigastrium and periumbilical region. Mild to moderate severity, associated with nausea, temporarily relieved by medications. No radiation. No vomiting, hematemesis, melena, constipation, diarrhea, distension, jaundice, or burning micturition.
Fever: Onset simultaneous with pain, intermittent (2 episodes/day), mild-to-moderate grade, with chills and rigors. No documented temp or diurnal variation. Relieved by meds. No cough, urinary symptoms, skin/dental infection, or altered sensorium. Undiagnosed despite treatment.
Menstrual History: Bleeding continues almost throughout the month, stopping only for 7-8 days. Moderate-to-heavy flow (uses 4-5 pads/day). No dysmenorrhea, clots, foul discharge, post-coital bleeding, or previous similar episodes.
Generalized Swelling: Duration 2 months. Initially involved feet, progressed to face and whole body. Gradually progressive. No oliguria, frothy urine, orthopnea, PND, or breathlessness.
Generalized Weakness: Duration 2 months. Progressively worsening, difficulty with household work, easy fatigability.
Constitutional Symptoms: Marked anorexia for 1 year, significant weight loss over 1 year. Long-standing pica (eating uncooked rice for 3 years). Strongly suggests chronic iron deficiency anemia.

Past History:
Similar complaints 1 year back. Admitted to Santosh Hospital with fever, pain, and swelling. Received 4 units of PRBC transfusion. Bone marrow aspiration and biopsy performed. Referred to AIIMS for splenectomy evaluation. Discontinued treatment after 3-4 months voluntarily and lost to follow-up. No history of DM, HTN, TB, CLD, hepatitis, or previous surgery.

Drug History: Intermittent symptomatic meds. No long-term NSAID, anticoagulant, or herbal use.

Family History: No family history of hemolytic anemia or blood disorders.

[PHASE 1 — HISTORY PRESENTATION

]Q1 Give a one-sentence problem representation of this case.

MODEL_ANS1 A 21-year-old unmarried female has a chronic multisystem illness with abdominal pain, intermittent fever, excessive menstrual bleeding, generalized edema, weakness, weight loss, anorexia, and long-standing pica, with previous severe anemia requiring packed red cell transfusion; the key syndromic possibilities are severe iron deficiency anemia due to chronic blood loss, anemia with hepatosplenomegaly/hypersplenism, chronic infection such as malaria or kala-azar, hematological malignancy, or chronic inflammatory disease. Kundu emphasizes that history should be structured into chief complaints, present illness, past, family, personal/social, treatment, and menstrual history in women.

Q2 What additional points must be asked in the history of abdominal pain?

MODEL_ANS2 You should ask site, radiation, character, severity, onset, progression, aggravating and relieving factors, relation to food, vomiting, hematemesis, melena, bowel habit, abdominal distension, jaundice, urinary symptoms, and gynecological causes. Alagappan lists important causes of abdominal pain as peritoneal inflammation, hollow viscus obstruction, vascular causes, abdominal wall causes, distension of visceral capsules, referred pain, metabolic causes, and neurogenic causes.

Q3 How will you analyze the fever in this patient?

MODEL_ANS3 Fever should be characterized by documented temperature, pattern, chills/rigors, sweating, diurnal variation, periodicity, response to antipyretics, associated cough, urinary symptoms, skin/dental infection, altered sensorium, weight loss, lymphadenopathy, bleeding, travel/residence in endemic areas, and prior antibiotics or antimalarials. Alagappan describes continuous, remittent, intermittent, relapsing, tertian, quartan, Pel-Ebstein, and saddle-back fever patterns.

Q4 What differential diagnoses are suggested by fever with weight loss and possible splenic disease?

MODEL_ANS4 Important differentials include chronic malaria, chronic kala-azar, tuberculosis, lymphoma, leukemia, cirrhosis with portal hypertension, hemolytic anemia including thalassemia, and chronic inflammatory disease. Kundu lists chronic malaria, chronic kala-azar, thalassemia, cirrhosis, lymphoma, leukemia, disseminated tuberculosis, SLE, infectious mononucleosis, AIDS, and related infections among causes of hepatosplenomegaly.

Q5 Why is the history of pica important here?

MODEL_ANS5 Long-standing pica strongly supports iron deficiency anemia, particularly when associated with chronic blood loss such as menorrhagia. Kundu describes pica as compulsive eating of non-nutrient substances and lists it among the clinical features of iron deficiency anemia; Alagappan also lists pica, koilonychia, glossitis, brittle hair, Plummer-Vinson syndrome, and menorrhagia among clinical features of iron deficiency anemia.

Q6 How should the excessive menstrual bleeding be quantified?

MODEL_ANS6 Ask age at menarche, previous cycle regularity, duration of bleeding, cycle length, number of pads per day, flooding, passage of clots, dysmenorrhea, intermenstrual bleeding, postcoital bleeding, foul discharge, last menstrual period, pregnancy possibility, contraception, sexual history where appropriate, and previous gynecological evaluation. Kundu specifically states that in anemia, full menstrual history with amount of blood loss must be asked.

Q7 What are the common causes of iron deficiency anemia relevant to this patient?

MODEL_ANS7 Relevant causes include nutritional deficiency, chronic menstrual blood loss, bleeding peptic ulcer, NSAID-related gastric erosion, hemorrhoids, hookworm infestation, malabsorption, pregnancy/lactation, and adolescence. Kundu lists menorrhagia, GI blood loss, hookworm, malabsorption, nutritional deficiency, pregnancy, lactation, and adolescence as common causes of iron deficiency anemia.

Q8 How will you interpret generalized swelling in this case?

MODEL_ANS8 Generalized swelling beginning in the feet and progressing to face/body may suggest cardiac, renal, hepatic, nutritional, severe anemia-related, or hypoalbuminemic edema. Absence of oliguria/frothy urine reduces but does not exclude renal disease; absence of orthopnea/PND/breathlessness reduces cardiac failure but severe anemia can still produce high-output failure. Kundu differentiates cardiac edema as dependent edema beginning at ankles, nephrotic edema as early-morning periorbital edema, cirrhotic edema as ascites first, and nutritional edema as feet with facial puffiness.

Q9 Why was it important to ask about NSAID and anticoagulant use?

MODEL_ANS9 NSAIDs may cause gastric erosion, peptic ulceration, and occult GI blood loss contributing to iron deficiency anemia; anticoagulants can worsen menstrual or gastrointestinal bleeding. Kundu includes NSAID intake in anemia history and Alagappan lists gastric erosion by NSAIDs and GI bleeding among causes of iron deficiency anemia.

Q10 What is the importance of previous packed red cell transfusion?

MODEL_ANS10 It indicates prior severe anemia or acute decompensation and requires documentation of number of units, indication, pre- and post-transfusion hemoglobin, transfusion reactions, blood group, screening status, and post-transfusion hepatitis/HIV risk. Alagappan states that severe anemia is an indication for transfusion and that one unit of RBCs raises hemoglobin by about 1 g/dL in an average adult.

Q11 What should you ask about the previous bone marrow aspiration and biopsy?

MODEL_ANS11 Ask why it was done, reports of cellularity, iron stores, erythroid/myeloid series, blasts, hemophagocytosis, parasites such as LD bodies, marrow infiltration, fibrosis, aplasia, and whether it explained the referral for splenectomy. Kundu notes that kala-azar diagnosis can be supported by demonstration of LD bodies in bone marrow smear or splenic puncture, and Alagappan classifies anemia by marrow response, maturation disorder, marrow damage, and hemolysis/blood loss.

Q12 Why was she referred for splenectomy, and what syndrome must you consider?

MODEL_ANS12 Referral for splenectomy suggests suspected hypersplenism, massive symptomatic splenomegaly, or a hematological disorder. Hypersplenism requires splenomegaly, cytopenia affecting one or more cell lines, normal or hypercellular marrow, and reversibility after splenectomy. Kundu gives these criteria and lists secondary causes including lymphoma, myeloproliferative disorders, connective tissue disease, cirrhosis, and congestive splenomegaly.

Q13 What occupational and social history is missing?

MODEL_ANS13 You should ask occupation, barefoot walking, sanitation, source of drinking water, diet, socioeconomic status, menstrual hygiene, travel/residence in malaria or kala-azar endemic areas, animal exposure, smoking/alcohol/substance use, and exposure to chemicals such as lead or benzene. Kundu emphasizes personal, dietary, occupational, geographic, and ethnic history in anemia, including barefoot walking for hookworm and chemical exposure for marrow disease.

Q14 What bleeding history must be asked beyond menstruation?

MODEL_ANS14 Ask epistaxis, gum bleeding, easy bruising, petechiae, prolonged bleeding after cuts, hematuria, melena, hematemesis, bleeding per rectum, family history of bleeding disorders, and drug use. Kundu includes bleeding tendencies, chronic GI blood loss, hemorrhoids, menorrhagia, and stool occult blood/ova examination in the evaluation of anemia.

Q15 What provisional diagnosis can be made from history alone?

MODEL_ANS15 The safest provisional diagnosis is chronic severe anemia, likely iron deficiency anemia due to excessive menstrual blood loss, with edema and constitutional symptoms requiring evaluation for associated chronic infection, hepatosplenomegaly/hypersplenism, hematological malignancy, or chronic inflammatory disease. Kundu states chronic blood loss is globally a common cause of anemia and lists menorrhagia among common causes; however, fever, weight loss, prior marrow study, transfusion, and splenectomy referral demand evaluation for kala-azar, malaria, thalassemia/hemolysis, leukemia/lymphoma, and hypersplenism.

]Q1 What essential points of general examination were not stated?

MODEL_ANS1 You should state consciousness, comfort/decubitus, pulse, BP, respiratory rate, temperature, pallor grading, icterus, cyanosis, clubbing, koilonychia, lymph nodes, JVP, edema, skin/nail/hair changes, hydration, height, weight, and BMI. Kundu’s general survey specifically includes build, nutrition/BMI, pallor, jaundice, JVP, lymph nodes, clubbing, koilonychia, pulse, respiration, temperature, BP, edema, skin, hair, and nails.

Q2 How should pallor be examined and graded?

MODEL_ANS2 Pallor should be looked for in lower palpebral conjunctiva, tongue, oral mucosa, nail beds, palmar creases, and soles, and then graded clinically as mild, moderate, or severe. In this case, pallor supports anemia, but the severity cannot be inferred unless you state the sites and degree. Kundu advises recording pallor/anemia as mild, moderate, or severe.

Q3 What is the significance of BMI 17 in this case?

MODEL_ANS3 BMI 17 indicates undernutrition/thin build. In this clinical context, it may reflect chronic illness, chronic infection such as kala-azar or tuberculosis, malignancy such as lymphoma/leukemia, malabsorption, poor dietary intake, or severe iron deficiency anemia with anorexia and weight loss. Kundu includes nutrition assessment by BMI in the general survey.

Q4 What peripheral signs of iron deficiency anemia should be looked for?

MODEL_ANS4 Look for pallor, koilonychia or platonychia, brittle nails, glossitis, angular stomatitis, cheilosis, brittle hair, and signs of Plummer-Vinson syndrome. Kundu lists pica/chronic blood loss, pallor, glossitis, angular stomatitis, brittle or spoon-shaped nails, brittle hair, dysphagia, and rarely mild splenomegaly as clinical clues to iron deficiency anemia.

Q5 What cardiovascular signs of severe anemia must be examined?

MODEL_ANS5 Examine pulse for tachycardia or collapsing character, BP for wide pulse pressure, apex for hyperdynamic impulse/cardiomegaly, and auscultate for hemic systolic murmurs. Also look for ankle edema, basal crepitations, and evidence of heart failure. Kundu describes tachycardia, high systolic/low diastolic BP, water-hammer pulse, venous hum, cardiomegaly, hyperdynamic apex, functional murmurs, edema, and basal crepitations in severe anemia.

Q6 How should the spleen be described?

MODEL_ANS6 Describe the distance below the left costal margin in centimeters, direction of enlargement, margin, notch, surface, consistency, tenderness, mobility with respiration, percussion over Traube’s space, presence of splenic rub, and whether it crosses the midline or reaches the umbilicus/pelvis. Merely saying “moderate splenomegaly” is incomplete. Kundu’s abdominal proforma requires detailed palpation of spleen and percussion of splenic dullness/Traube’s space.

Q7 How do you clinically differentiate splenomegaly from a left renal mass?

MODEL_ANS7 A spleen enlarges inferomedially, moves with respiration, has a notch, cannot be insinuated above, is dull to percussion, and has no band of colonic resonance over it. A renal mass is bimanually palpable, ballotable, may have colonic resonance anteriorly, and one can usually insinuate above it. Kundu specifically includes spleen, kidneys, percussion, and the band of colonic resonance over a kidney lump in abdominal examination.

Q8 What causes of moderate splenomegaly fit this case?

MODEL_ANS8 The important causes here are chronic malaria, chronic kala-azar, lymphoma, leukemia, hemolytic anemia/thalassemia, cirrhosis with portal hypertension, SLE, and enteric fever. Kundu lists chronic malaria with moderate-to-massive firm spleen, kala-azar with massive soft-to-firm spleen, thalassemia/hemolysis, cirrhosis, CML, lymphoma, enteric fever, and SLE among differentials of hepatosplenomegaly.

Q9 Why is “moderate splenomegaly” not enough for diagnosis?

MODEL_ANS9 Because the consistency and tenderness refine the differential: chronic malaria tends to have firm-to-hard non-tender splenomegaly; kala-azar often has soft-to-firm non-tender spleen; CML may have huge hard spleen with possible rub; lymphoma commonly has moderate non-tender splenomegaly; enteric fever may have a soft tender just palpable spleen. Kundu emphasizes these differences in the differential diagnosis of hepatosplenomegaly.

Q10 What liver findings should you have presented?

MODEL_ANS10 You should state whether liver is enlarged, its span, edge, surface, consistency, tenderness, pulsatility, and whether there are signs of chronic liver disease such as jaundice, spider naevi, palmar erythema, ascites, abdominal wall veins, or splenomegaly from portal hypertension. Kundu’s abdominal examination requires detailed liver examination and assessment for ascites, venous prominences, and hepatosplenomegaly.

Q11 What lymphoreticular examination is mandatory in this case?

MODEL_ANS11 Examine all lymph node groups, liver and spleen, sternum for tenderness, oral cavity, skin for hemorrhagic manifestations, and fundus for retinal hemorrhage or papilledema. Kundu’s lymphoreticular proforma includes anemia/jaundice, hemorrhagic manifestations, oral cavity, detailed lymph nodes, liver and spleen, sternal tenderness, and ophthalmoscopy.

Q12 What abdominal examination should follow the finding of epigastric and umbilical tenderness?

MODEL_ANS12 State whether tenderness is superficial or deep, whether guarding/rigidity is present, whether rebound tenderness is present, and assess bowel sounds, organomegaly, masses, ascites, hernial sites, renal angles, and urinary bladder. Kundu states that painful areas should preferably be examined last and includes deep tender spots, rebound tenderness, shifting dullness, and bowel sounds in the abdominal proforma.

Q13 What signs of chronic kala-azar should be sought?

MODEL_ANS13 Look for emaciation, moderate anemia, dry rough pigmented skin, scanty scalp hair, protuberant abdomen, thin legs, occasional pedal edema, hepatosplenomegaly, late jaundice, crepitations from secondary infection, tachycardia, low BP, anemia-related murmur, gum bleeding or epistaxis, and rarely sternal tenderness or lymph nodes. Kundu lists these physical findings in chronic kala-azar.

Q14 What negative findings should be specifically stated in a suspected anemia with splenomegaly?

MODEL_ANS14 State absence or presence of jaundice, lymphadenopathy, sternal tenderness, bony tenderness, petechiae/purpura, gum bleeding, pedal edema, ascites, hepatomegaly, abdominal wall veins, signs of heart failure, and signs of chronic liver disease. Kundu’s lymphoreticular proforma and severe anemia section both stress hemorrhagic manifestations, lymph nodes, liver/spleen, sternal tenderness, CVS signs, edema, and hepatosplenomegaly.

Q15 Give your clinical impression from the examination findings alone.

MODEL_ANS15 Examination suggests an undernourished young female with pallor and moderate splenomegaly, with epigastric/umbilical tenderness. This supports anemia with splenomegaly rather than simple isolated iron deficiency alone, because iron deficiency usually has only mild splenomegaly rarely. The working possibilities remain severe nutritional/iron deficiency anemia with another cause of splenomegaly, chronic malaria, kala-azar, hemolytic anemia/thalassemia, lymphoma/leukemia, or portal hypertension-related splenomegaly.

[PHASE 2 — PHYSICAL EXAMINATION PRESENTATION

1]. Probing Section Format:

Q1 You noted the presence of pallor. What are the specific anatomical sites you examined to determine the severity of pallor, and how do you clinically differentiate mild, moderate, and severe pallor?
MODEL_ANS1 Pallor is examined in the lower palpebral conjunctiva, palmar creases, bulbar conjunctiva, oral mucosa (underside of the tongue), and nail beds. Clinically, according to Kundu's Bedside Clinics in Medicine, loss of pinkish tinge indicates mild pallor; severe pallor is characterized by complete blanching of the palmar creases or a pearly white appearance of the conjunctiva, usually correlating with a hemoglobin level below 7 g/dL.

Q2 The patient has generalized swelling (anasarca) mentioned in the history, but your positive examination findings do not specify the nature of the edema. How did you test for edema, and what is its physiological type in this patient?
MODEL_ANS2 Edema is tested by applying firm digital pressure with the thumb over a bony prominence (like the medial malleolus, anterior tibia, or sacrum) for at least 15 seconds. In this patient, the edema is expected to be pitting in nature, caused by a hyperdynamic circulatory state from chronic severe anemia (increased hydrostatic capillary filtration) combined with hypoalbuminemia from malnutrition (reduced oncotic pressure). (Reference: Kundu, Part I, General Examination).

Q3 You noted a thin built with a BMI of 17. How do you distinguish between "built" and "nutrition" during a general physical survey?
MODEL_ANS3 "Built" refers to the inherited skeletal framework of the body, which can be categorized as average, tall, or short stature. "Nutrition" refers to the status of nourishment, reflecting the amount of subcutaneous fat and muscle mass. A patient can have an average built but poor nutrition (underweight/malnourished). (Reference: Kundu, Part I, General Survey).

Q4 The patient has a history of high-grade fever. Did you look for any peripheral signs of infective endocarditis or systemic emboli during your general examination?
MODEL_ANS4 In a patient with chronic pyrexia, severe anemia, and splenomegaly, it is mandatory to rule out infective endocarditis by checking for peripheral stigmata: Osler's nodes (tender nodules on finger pads), Janeway lesions (non-tender macules on palms/soles), splinter hemorrhages in the nail beds, petechiae, and Roth's spots on fundoscopy. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine).

Q5 In the history, the patient complained of excessive menstrual bleeding. What specific cutaneous or mucosal findings should you look for to exclude a generalized bleeding diathesis?
MODEL_ANS5 To rule out a primary bleeding disorder or thrombocytopenia (which could explain both the polymenometrorrhagia and potential visceral bleeding), one must carefully inspect the skin and mucous membranes for petechiae (pinpoint hemorrhagic spots), purpura (larger macules), ecchymoses (bruises), and bleeding gums. (Reference: Kundu, Part III, Hematological Survey).

Q6 You mentioned "moderate splenomegaly" extending below the left costal margin. What are the precise clinical features that confirm a palpable abdominal mass is indeed the spleen and not the left kidney?
MODEL_ANS6 A palpable spleen is characterized by: an inability to get above the mass, a distinct notch on its anterior border, movement obliquely downward and medially towards the right iliac fossa during inspiration, a sharp edge, and dullness to percussion over the mass without overlying resonant bowel gas (which is typical for a retroperitoneal kidney). (Reference: Kundu, Part I, Abdomen Examination).

Q7 How do you measure and document the size of the spleen in centimeters, and what is the landmark used?
MODEL_ANS7 Spleen enlargement is measured in centimeters from the left costal margin at the mid-clavicular line to the tip of the spleen along the axis of its enlargement (towards the right iliac fossa) at the end of deep inspiration. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine).

Q8 Given the epigastric and umbilical tenderness, how do you perform superficial versus deep palpation to distinguish abdominal wall tenderness from visceral tenderness?
MODEL_ANS8 Superficial palpation is done gently using the flat of the hand to detect muscle guarding, rigidity, and hyperesthesia of the abdominal wall. Deep palpation applies greater pressure to identify organomegaly or localized deep visceral tenderness. If pain increases when the patient tenses their abdominal muscles (Carnett's sign), the tenderness is inside the abdominal wall; if it decreases, it is visceral. (Reference: Kundu, Part I, Abdomen Examination).

Q9 In a patient presenting with pyrexia, severe anemia, and moderate splenomegaly, what is the significance of checking for sternal tenderness?
MODEL_ANS9 Sternal tenderness is evaluated by applying firm pressure with the thumb over the body of the sternum. Severe pain or tenderness indicates expansion of the bone marrow, which is highly characteristic of acute leukemias, chronic myeloid leukemia, or severe megaloblastic anemia. (Reference: Kundu, Part I, Lymphoreticular System).

Q10 You documented abdominal tenderness but did not comment on ascites. Which specific percussion techniques must be performed to reliably evaluate for free fluid in the peritoneal cavity?
MODEL_ANS10 To demonstrate free fluid (ascites), one must perform: percussion for shifting dullness (for moderate fluid, usually > 500 mL) and the fluid thrill test (for massive, tense ascites). For very minimal fluid, the puddle sign can be attempted. (Reference: Kundu, Part I, Abdomen Examination).

Q11 Chronic iron deficiency anemia can lead to a hyperdynamic circulatory state. What specific auscultatory findings do you look for in the cardiovascular system in such a patient?
MODEL_ANS11 A severe, long-standing anemia causes a hyperdynamic state, which clinically manifests as a loud first heart sound (S1​), a functional/hemic ejection systolic murmur loudest over the pulmonary area (grade 2-3/6, non-radiating), and occasionally a venous hum in the neck. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine).

Q12 Why is it necessary to perform a digital rectal examination (P/R examination) in a patient with profound iron deficiency anemia and abdominal pain?
MODEL_ANS12 A digital rectal examination is vital to inspect for occult or frank gastrointestinal blood loss (melena, fresh blood), rectal masses, hemorrhoids, or fissures that could serve as the source of chronic blood loss causing severe iron deficiency anemia. (Reference: Kundu, Part I, Abdomen Examination).

Q13 You noted moderate splenomegaly. Did you examine for hepatomegaly, and why is the presence or absence of hepatomegaly critical for narrowing down the diagnosis?
MODEL_ANS13 Concomitant hepatomegaly (hepatosplenomegaly) must be checked by assessing the liver span. Isolated splenomegaly or disproportionate splenomegaly points towards portal hypertension (pre-hepatic/sinusoidal), chronic malaria, Kala-azar, or hematological malignancies, whereas combined hepatosplenomegaly often indicates chronic liver disease, storage disorders, or disseminated tuberculosis. (Reference: Kundu, Part I, Abdomen Examination).

Q14 The patient has a history of prolonged fever and abdominal pain. What is the clinical significance of checking for palpable lymph nodes in the cervical, axillary, and inguinal regions?
MODEL_ANS14 Generalised lymphadenopathy in the presence of fever and splenomegaly would narrow the diagnosis down to systemic conditions like lymphoma, leukemia, disseminated tuberculosis, or visceral leishmaniasis. Their absence helps keep the focus localized to abdominal or primary hematological processes. (Reference: Kundu, Part I, Lymphoreticular System).

Q15 How do you clinically assess the jugular venous pressure (JVP) in this patient, and what finding do you expect given her generalized swelling?
MODEL_ANS15 JVP is assessed with the patient reclining at a 45-degree angle, inspecting the internal jugular vein pulsations. In severe nutritional anemia or volume overload from high-output cardiac failure, the JVP may be elevated. If the swelling is purely due to nutritional hypoalbuminemia without heart failure, the JVP will be normal or low. (Reference: Kundu, Part I, General Examination).
