Chief Complaints:
  • Abdominal pain for 3 months
  • Fever for 3 months
  • Excessive menstrual bleeding / polymenometrorrhagia for 3 months
  • Generalized swelling for 2 months

History of Present Illness:
The patient was asymptomatic around 3 months back, when she developed abdominal pain.
Abdominal Pain: Insidious onset, intermittent, colicky, located in epigastrium and periumbilical region. Mild to moderate severity, associated with nausea, temporarily relieved by medications. No radiation. No vomiting, hematemesis, melena, constipation, diarrhea, distension, jaundice, or burning micturition.
Fever: Onset simultaneous with pain, intermittent (2 episodes/day), mild-to-moderate grade, with chills and rigors. No documented temp or diurnal variation. Relieved by meds. No cough, urinary symptoms, skin/dental infection, or altered sensorium. Undiagnosed despite treatment.
Menstrual History: Bleeding continues almost throughout the month, stopping only for 7-8 days. Moderate-to-heavy flow (uses 4-5 pads/day). No dysmenorrhea, clots, foul discharge, post-coital bleeding, or previous similar episodes.
Generalized Swelling: Duration 2 months. Initially involved feet, progressed to face and whole body. Gradually progressive. No oliguria, frothy urine, orthopnea, PND, or breathlessness.
Generalized Weakness: Duration 2 months. Progressively worsening, difficulty with household work, easy fatigability.
Constitutional Symptoms: Marked anorexia for 1 year, significant weight loss over 1 year. Long-standing pica (eating uncooked rice for 3 years). Strongly suggests chronic iron deficiency anemia.

Past History:
Similar complaints 1 year back. Admitted to Santosh Hospital with fever, pain, and swelling. Received 4 units of PRBC transfusion. Bone marrow aspiration and biopsy performed. Referred to AIIMS for splenectomy evaluation. Discontinued treatment after 3-4 months voluntarily and lost to follow-up. No history of DM, HTN, TB, CLD, hepatitis, or previous surgery.

Drug History: Intermittent symptomatic meds. No long-term NSAID, anticoagulant, or herbal use.

Family History: No family history of hemolytic anemia or blood disorders.

[PHASE 1 — HISTORY PRESENTATION

]Q1 Give a one-sentence problem representation of this case.

MODEL_ANS1 A 21-year-old unmarried female has a chronic multisystem illness with abdominal pain, intermittent fever, excessive menstrual bleeding, generalized edema, weakness, weight loss, anorexia, and long-standing pica, with previous severe anemia requiring packed red cell transfusion; the key syndromic possibilities are severe iron deficiency anemia due to chronic blood loss, anemia with hepatosplenomegaly/hypersplenism, chronic infection such as malaria or kala-azar, hematological malignancy, or chronic inflammatory disease. Kundu emphasizes that history should be structured into chief complaints, present illness, past, family, personal/social, treatment, and menstrual history in women.

Q2 What additional points must be asked in the history of abdominal pain?

MODEL_ANS2 You should ask site, radiation, character, severity, onset, progression, aggravating and relieving factors, relation to food, vomiting, hematemesis, melena, bowel habit, abdominal distension, jaundice, urinary symptoms, and gynecological causes. Alagappan lists important causes of abdominal pain as peritoneal inflammation, hollow viscus obstruction, vascular causes, abdominal wall causes, distension of visceral capsules, referred pain, metabolic causes, and neurogenic causes.

Q3 How will you analyze the fever in this patient?

MODEL_ANS3 Fever should be characterized by documented temperature, pattern, chills/rigors, sweating, diurnal variation, periodicity, response to antipyretics, associated cough, urinary symptoms, skin/dental infection, altered sensorium, weight loss, lymphadenopathy, bleeding, travel/residence in endemic areas, and prior antibiotics or antimalarials. Alagappan describes continuous, remittent, intermittent, relapsing, tertian, quartan, Pel-Ebstein, and saddle-back fever patterns.

Q4 What differential diagnoses are suggested by fever with weight loss and possible splenic disease?

MODEL_ANS4 Important differentials include chronic malaria, chronic kala-azar, tuberculosis, lymphoma, leukemia, cirrhosis with portal hypertension, hemolytic anemia including thalassemia, and chronic inflammatory disease. Kundu lists chronic malaria, chronic kala-azar, thalassemia, cirrhosis, lymphoma, leukemia, disseminated tuberculosis, SLE, infectious mononucleosis, AIDS, and related infections among causes of hepatosplenomegaly.

Q5 Why is the history of pica important here?

MODEL_ANS5 Long-standing pica strongly supports iron deficiency anemia, particularly when associated with chronic blood loss such as menorrhagia. Kundu describes pica as compulsive eating of non-nutrient substances and lists it among the clinical features of iron deficiency anemia; Alagappan also lists pica, koilonychia, glossitis, brittle hair, Plummer-Vinson syndrome, and menorrhagia among clinical features of iron deficiency anemia.

Q6 How should the excessive menstrual bleeding be quantified?

MODEL_ANS6 Ask age at menarche, previous cycle regularity, duration of bleeding, cycle length, number of pads per day, flooding, passage of clots, dysmenorrhea, intermenstrual bleeding, postcoital bleeding, foul discharge, last menstrual period, pregnancy possibility, contraception, sexual history where appropriate, and previous gynecological evaluation. Kundu specifically states that in anemia, full menstrual history with amount of blood loss must be asked.

Q7 What are the common causes of iron deficiency anemia relevant to this patient?

MODEL_ANS7 Relevant causes include nutritional deficiency, chronic menstrual blood loss, bleeding peptic ulcer, NSAID-related gastric erosion, hemorrhoids, hookworm infestation, malabsorption, pregnancy/lactation, and adolescence. Kundu lists menorrhagia, GI blood loss, hookworm, malabsorption, nutritional deficiency, pregnancy, lactation, and adolescence as common causes of iron deficiency anemia.

Q8 How will you interpret generalized swelling in this case?

MODEL_ANS8 Generalized swelling beginning in the feet and progressing to face/body may suggest cardiac, renal, hepatic, nutritional, severe anemia-related, or hypoalbuminemic edema. Absence of oliguria/frothy urine reduces but does not exclude renal disease; absence of orthopnea/PND/breathlessness reduces cardiac failure but severe anemia can still produce high-output failure. Kundu differentiates cardiac edema as dependent edema beginning at ankles, nephrotic edema as early-morning periorbital edema, cirrhotic edema as ascites first, and nutritional edema as feet with facial puffiness.

Q9 Why was it important to ask about NSAID and anticoagulant use?

MODEL_ANS9 NSAIDs may cause gastric erosion, peptic ulceration, and occult GI blood loss contributing to iron deficiency anemia; anticoagulants can worsen menstrual or gastrointestinal bleeding. Kundu includes NSAID intake in anemia history and Alagappan lists gastric erosion by NSAIDs and GI bleeding among causes of iron deficiency anemia.

Q10 What is the importance of previous packed red cell transfusion?

MODEL_ANS10 It indicates prior severe anemia or acute decompensation and requires documentation of number of units, indication, pre- and post-transfusion hemoglobin, transfusion reactions, blood group, screening status, and post-transfusion hepatitis/HIV risk. Alagappan states that severe anemia is an indication for transfusion and that one unit of RBCs raises hemoglobin by about 1 g/dL in an average adult.

Q11 What should you ask about the previous bone marrow aspiration and biopsy?

MODEL_ANS11 Ask why it was done, reports of cellularity, iron stores, erythroid/myeloid series, blasts, hemophagocytosis, parasites such as LD bodies, marrow infiltration, fibrosis, aplasia, and whether it explained the referral for splenectomy. Kundu notes that kala-azar diagnosis can be supported by demonstration of LD bodies in bone marrow smear or splenic puncture, and Alagappan classifies anemia by marrow response, maturation disorder, marrow damage, and hemolysis/blood loss.

Q12 Why was she referred for splenectomy, and what syndrome must you consider?

MODEL_ANS12 Referral for splenectomy suggests suspected hypersplenism, massive symptomatic splenomegaly, or a hematological disorder. Hypersplenism requires splenomegaly, cytopenia affecting one or more cell lines, normal or hypercellular marrow, and reversibility after splenectomy. Kundu gives these criteria and lists secondary causes including lymphoma, myeloproliferative disorders, connective tissue disease, cirrhosis, and congestive splenomegaly.

Q13 What occupational and social history is missing?

MODEL_ANS13 You should ask occupation, barefoot walking, sanitation, source of drinking water, diet, socioeconomic status, menstrual hygiene, travel/residence in malaria or kala-azar endemic areas, animal exposure, smoking/alcohol/substance use, and exposure to chemicals such as lead or benzene. Kundu emphasizes personal, dietary, occupational, geographic, and ethnic history in anemia, including barefoot walking for hookworm and chemical exposure for marrow disease.

Q14 What bleeding history must be asked beyond menstruation?

MODEL_ANS14 Ask epistaxis, gum bleeding, easy bruising, petechiae, prolonged bleeding after cuts, hematuria, melena, hematemesis, bleeding per rectum, family history of bleeding disorders, and drug use. Kundu includes bleeding tendencies, chronic GI blood loss, hemorrhoids, menorrhagia, and stool occult blood/ova examination in the evaluation of anemia.

Q15 What provisional diagnosis can be made from history alone?

MODEL_ANS15 The safest provisional diagnosis is chronic severe anemia, likely iron deficiency anemia due to excessive menstrual blood loss, with edema and constitutional symptoms requiring evaluation for associated chronic infection, hepatosplenomegaly/hypersplenism, hematological malignancy, or chronic inflammatory disease. Kundu states chronic blood loss is globally a common cause of anemia and lists menorrhagia among common causes; however, fever, weight loss, prior marrow study, transfusion, and splenectomy referral demand evaluation for kala-azar, malaria, thalassemia/hemolysis, leukemia/lymphoma, and hypersplenism.

1. Probing Section Format:

Q1 You mentioned the patient has black-colored stool for 15 days. Clinically, how do you differentiate melena from spurious melena (e.g., from iron or bismuth supplements) based strictly on history?
MODEL_ANS1 Melena is typically jet-black, tarry, sticky, and has a characteristic offensive or foul smell due to the degradation of blood by intestinal bacteria. Stools darkened by oral iron or bismuth are usually not tarry, sticky, or foul-smelling. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Gastrointestinal System).

Q2 The patient developed lower limb swelling followed by abdominal distension. What is the usual sequence of edema in chronic liver disease versus cardiac failure?
MODEL_ANS2 In chronic liver disease, abdominal distension (ascites) typically precedes or appears simultaneously with lower limb edema due to portal hypertension and hypoalbuminemia. In congestive heart failure, dependent lower limb edema usually appears first, eventually progressing to ascites and anasarca. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 1, Gastrointestinal and Hepatobiliary System).

Q3 What are the primary pathophysiological mechanisms responsible for ascites and lower limb swelling in a patient with advanced liver disease?
MODEL_ANS3 The primary mechanisms include portal hypertension (leading to splanchnic vasodilation and increased capillary hydrostatic pressure) and reduced hepatic synthesis of albumin causing decreased plasma oncotic pressure. Secondary hyperaldosteronism also causes sodium and water retention. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Diseases of the Liver).

Q4 In a patient presenting with melena and a background of liver injury (DILI or cirrhosis), what is the most likely and dangerous source of gastrointestinal bleeding?
MODEL_ANS4 The most common and life-threatening source in patients with chronic liver disease or portal hypertension is ruptured esophageal or gastric varices. Other sources include portal hypertensive gastropathy or peptic ulcer disease. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Diseases of the Liver).

Q5 You correctly noted the absence of facial puffiness and oliguria. Why are these relevant negatives in this specific presentation?
MODEL_ANS5 Facial puffiness (especially periorbital edema primarily noticed in the morning) and oliguria are classic historical features of primary renal pathology, such as nephrotic or nephritic syndrome, which cause hypoproteinemia or fluid retention. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 1, Nephrology).

Q6 The patient has a history of Anti-Tubercular Therapy (ATT). Which specific first-line ATT drugs are primarily responsible for Drug-Induced Liver Injury (DILI)?
MODEL_ANS6 The first-line ATT drugs most commonly causing hepatotoxicity are Isoniazid (H), Rifampicin (R), and Pyrazinamide (Z). (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 1, Respiratory System).

Q7 What vital negative history must you elicit in any patient presenting with 15 days of gastrointestinal bleeding to assess for hemodynamic compromise?
MODEL_ANS7 It is crucial to ask about symptoms of acute anemia and hypovolemia, such as postural dizziness, syncope, palpitations, excessive fatigue, and breathlessness on exertion. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Gastrointestinal System).

Q8 If this patient's liver dysfunction progresses, what early historical symptoms would suggest the development of hepatic encephalopathy?
MODEL_ANS8 Early symptoms of hepatic encephalopathy include an altered sleep rhythm (reversal of the sleep-wake cycle), behavioral changes, apathy, memory impairment, and eventually confusion. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 1, Hepatobiliary System).

Q9 You described the lower limb swelling as "painful." What common complication in a dependent, edematous limb can lead to painful swelling, and how would you distinguish it historically?
MODEL_ANS9 Painful edema can suggest superimposed cellulitis or deep vein thrombosis (DVT). A history of sudden worsening of swelling in one leg, severe localized pain, redness, or fever would point towards cellulitis or DVT rather than simple bilateral transudative edema. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 2).

Q10 What questions should be asked to differentiate the "yellowish discoloration" (jaundice) of hepatocellular injury from obstructive (cholestatic) jaundice?
MODEL_ANS10 One should ask about the presence of intense generalized pruritus (itching), high-colored urine, and clay-colored (pale) stools, which strongly suggest an obstructive etiology. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Diseases of the Liver).

Q11 The patient has a known history of hypothyroidism. Can hypothyroidism cause edema? If so, what is its characteristic physical feature?
MODEL_ANS11 Yes, severe hypothyroidism can cause myxedema. It is characteristically a non-pitting type of edema due to the interstitial accumulation of hydrophilic mucopolysaccharides, although early forms can sometimes have a mild pitting component. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 1, Endocrinology).

Q12 In a patient with melena, why is it important to ask specifically about a history of NSAID use or alternative medications?
MODEL_ANS12 NSAIDs are a major cause of erosive gastritis and peptic ulcer disease, leading to upper gastrointestinal bleeding. Alternative medications may contain hepatotoxic elements or hidden NSAIDs that exacerbate liver injury and bleeding risk. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Gastrointestinal System).

Q13 This patient had Tuberculosis. What form of abdominal TB could present with abdominal distension, and how does its history differ from cirrhotic ascites?
MODEL_ANS13 Tubercular peritonitis can present with ascites. It is often accompanied by a history of low-grade evening rise of fever, night sweats, significant weight loss, and chronic abdominal pain ("doughy abdomen"), rather than classic stigmata of chronic liver disease. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 1, Gastrointestinal System).

Q14 When taking a history of a patient with suspected chronic liver disease, what specific risk factors for viral hepatitis should you inquire about?
MODEL_ANS14 One should ask about a history of blood transfusions, intravenous drug use, unsafe sexual practices, needle stick injuries, and prior surgical or dental procedures using unsterilized equipment to rule out HBV and HCV. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Diseases of the Liver).

Q15 You noted her socioeconomic status using the Kuppuswamy scale. Why is socioeconomic history particularly relevant in a patient with a history of Tuberculosis?
MODEL_ANS15 Tuberculosis is closely linked to socioeconomic factors. Overcrowding, poor ventilation, undernutrition, and lack of access to early healthcare are significant risk factors for the acquisition, spread, and reactivation of TB. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part I, Respiratory System).

Q4 What peripheral signs of iron deficiency anemia should be looked for?

Kundu lists pica/chronic blood loss, pallor, glossitis, angular stomatitis, brittle or spoon-shaped nails, brittle hair, dysphagia, and rarely mild splenomegaly as clinical clues to iron deficiency anemia.

Q5 What cardiovascular signs of severe anemia must be examined?

MODEL_ANS5 Examine pulse for tachycardia or collapsing character, BP for wide pulse pressure, apex for hyperdynamic impulse/cardiomegaly, and auscultate for hemic systolic murmurs. Also look for ankle edema, basal crepitations, and evidence of heart failure. Kundu describes tachycardia, high systolic/low diastolic BP, water-hammer pulse, venous hum, cardiomegaly, hyperdynamic apex, functional murmurs, edema, and basal crepitations in severe anemia.

Q6 How should the spleen be described?

MODEL_ANS6 Describe the distance below the left costal margin in centimeters, direction of enlargement, margin, notch, surface, consistency, tenderness, mobility with respiration, percussion over Traube’s space, presence of splenic rub, and whether it crosses the midline or reaches the umbilicus/pelvis. Merely saying “moderate splenomegaly” is incomplete. Kundu’s abdominal proforma requires detailed palpation of spleen and percussion of splenic dullness/Traube’s space.

Q7 How do you clinically differentiate splenomegaly from a left renal mass?

MODEL_ANS7 A spleen enlarges inferomedially, moves with respiration, has a notch, cannot be insinuated above, is dull to percussion, and has no band of colonic resonance over it. A renal mass is bimanually palpable, ballotable, may have colonic resonance anteriorly, and one can usually insinuate above it. Kundu specifically includes spleen, kidneys, percussion, and the band of colonic resonance over a kidney lump in abdominal examination.

Q8 What causes of moderate splenomegaly fit this case?

MODEL_ANS8 The important causes here are chronic malaria, chronic kala-azar, lymphoma, leukemia, hemolytic anemia/thalassemia, cirrhosis with portal hypertension, SLE, and enteric fever. Kundu lists chronic malaria with moderate-to-massive firm spleen, kala-azar with massive soft-to-firm spleen, thalassemia/hemolysis, cirrhosis, CML, lymphoma, enteric fever, and SLE among differentials of hepatosplenomegaly.

Q9 Why is “moderate splenomegaly” not enough for diagnosis?

MODEL_ANS9 Because the consistency and tenderness refine the differential: chronic malaria tends to have firm-to-hard non-tender splenomegaly; kala-azar often has soft-to-firm non-tender spleen; CML may have huge hard spleen with possible rub; lymphoma commonly has moderate non-tender splenomegaly; enteric fever may have a soft tender just palpable spleen. Kundu emphasizes these differences in the differential diagnosis of hepatosplenomegaly.

MODEL_ANS10 You should state whether liver is enlarged, its span, edge, surface, consistency, tenderness, pulsatility, and whether there are signs of chronic liver disease such as jaundice, spider naevi, palmar erythema, ascites, abdominal wall veins, or splenomegaly from portal hypertension. Q11 What lymphoreticular examination is mandatory in this case?

MODEL_ANS11 Examine all lymph node groups, liver and spleen, sternum for tenderness, oral cavity, skin for hemorrhagic manifestations, and fundus for retinal hemorrhage or papilledema. Q12 What abdominal examination should follow the finding of epigastric and umbilical tenderness?

Q13 What signs of chronic kala-azar should be sought?

MODEL_ANS13 Look for emaciation, moderate anemia, dry rough pigmented skin, scanty scalp hair, protuberant abdomen, thin legs, occasional pedal edema, hepatosplenomegaly, late jaundice, crepitations from secondary infection, tachycardia, low BP, anemia-related murmur, gum bleeding or epistaxis, and rarely sternal tenderness or lymph nodes. Q14 What negative findings should be specifically stated in a suspected anemia with splenomegaly?

MODEL_ANS14 State absence or presence of jaundice, lymphadenopathy, sternal tenderness, bony tenderness, petechiae/purpura, gum bleeding, pedal edema, ascites, hepatomegaly, abdominal wall veins, signs of heart failure, and signs of chronic liver disease. ]Q1 You correctly identified the presence of pallor. What are the specific anatomical sites you must check to thoroughly evaluate for pallor in a patient?
MODEL_ANS1 Pallor should be routinely examined in the lower palpebral conjunctiva, dorsum of the tongue, soft palate, nail beds, and palmar creases. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 1, General Examination).

Q2 The patient has a BMI of 17. How do you categorize this BMI according to WHO classifications, and what does this finding suggest in the context of her chronic illness?
MODEL_ANS2 A BMI of 17 falls into the "Underweight" category (specifically, Grade II thinness or moderate malnutrition). In this patient, it suggests significant muscle and fat wasting (cachexia) secondary to chronic disease, such as chronic liver disease, chronic blood loss, or past tuberculosis. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 1, General Examination).

Q3 Given the 15-day history of melena and the finding of pallor, we strongly suspect iron deficiency anemia. What specific changes in the nails should you look for to support this chronicity?
MODEL_ANS3 You should look for platonychia (flat nails) or koilonychia (spoon-shaped nails), which are classic signs of chronic iron deficiency anemia. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 1, General Examination).

Q4 You noted "moderate splenomegaly." By clinical definition, how far does a moderately enlarged spleen extend below the left costal margin?
MODEL_ANS4 A moderately enlarged spleen extends between 4 cm and 8 cm below the left costal margin. Mild is less than 4 cm, and massive is greater than 8 cm (crossing the umbilicus or reaching the right iliac fossa). (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Abdomen).

Q5 During palpation, what is the normal trajectory or axis of splenic enlargement, and what anatomical structure dictates this?
MODEL_ANS5 The spleen enlarges diagonally towards the right iliac fossa (towards the umbilicus). This specific axis is dictated by the phrenicocolic ligament, which prevents it from enlarging straight downwards. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 1, Gastrointestinal System).

Q6 In a patient with a mass in the left hypochondrium, how do you clinically differentiate an enlarged spleen from an enlarged left kidney on palpation and percussion?
MODEL_ANS6 An enlarged spleen has a palpable notch, enlarges towards the right iliac fossa, is not ballotable, and you cannot get above its upper pole. Percussion over the spleen yields a dull note because it is anterior to the bowel, whereas an enlarged kidney is ballotable, has no notch, and is often resonant on percussion (due to overlying bowel). (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Abdomen).

Q7 The patient has a history of Drug-Induced Liver Injury (DILI). What is the most likely pathophysiological mechanism causing splenomegaly in this specific clinical scenario?
MODEL_ANS7 The most likely mechanism is portal hypertension secondary to liver architecture distortion (cirrhosis or progressive liver disease). Portal hypertension causes backward pressure in the portal venous system, leading to venous engorgement and congestive splenomegaly. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Diseases of the Liver).

Q8 The patient complained of abdominal distension. What specific percussion techniques must you perform to confirm the presence of free fluid (ascites) in the peritoneal cavity?
MODEL_ANS8 You must percuss for "shifting dullness" (for moderate ascites) and check for a "fluid thrill" (for tense, massive ascites). Puddle sign can be used for minimal ascites. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part 1, Gastrointestinal System).

Q9 You found tenderness over the epigastric and umbilical regions. Considering her history of melena, what intra-abdominal pathology does this tenderness point towards?
MODEL_ANS9 Epigastric tenderness in the setting of upper gastrointestinal bleeding (melena) strongly points towards peptic ulcer disease, erosive gastritis, or drug-induced (NSAID) gastropathy. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Gastrointestinal System).
