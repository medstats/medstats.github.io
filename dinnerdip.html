Hello, Chief Resident here.

This case presents an 85-year-old female with recurrent episodes of autonomic instability (sweating, palpitations, feeling cold) culminating in a syncopal event shortly after a meal. The combination of postprandial timing, constitutional symptoms (weight loss), and new-onset headaches in an elderly patient necessitates a broad differential ranging from metabolic disturbances to occult malignancy or vasculitis.

Key Findings:

  1. Postprandial Syncope: Loss of consciousness occurring 45 minutes after dinner.
  2. Autonomic Prodrome: Sweating, palpitations, and "feeling cold" suggest a sympathetic surge or a hypoglycemic response.
  3. Recurrent Nature: Three similar episodes in the last two weeks.
  4. Constitutional Symptoms: Unintentional weight loss (2-3 kg) over several months.
  5. Neurological Symptom: Recent onset of headaches.
  6. Age: 85 years old (high risk for cardiovascular events and malignancy).

Differential Diagnoses:

  1. Reactive Hypoglycemia (specifically Non-Insulinoma Pancreatogenous Hypoglycemia Syndrome - NIPHS or Insulinoma): (45%)
    The timing (45 minutes post-meal) is highly suggestive of postprandial hypoglycemia. While reactive hypoglycemia is often associated with gastric surgery (dumping syndrome), in an elderly patient with weight loss, we must consider an insulinoma or a paraneoplastic process producing insulin-like growth factors.

  2. Pheochromocytoma: (25%)
    The "classic triad" of palpitations, sweating, and headache, combined with weight loss, is highly suspicious. While syncope is less common than hypertension, orthostatic hypotension or arrhythmias associated with catecholamine surges can cause loss of consciousness.

  3. Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA): (15%)
    In an 85-year-old with new headaches and weight loss, GCA must be ruled out. While it doesn't typically cause syncope, systemic vasculitis can lead to various neurological presentations or be associated with other paraneoplastic phenomena.

  4. Cardiac Arrhythmia (e.g., Sick Sinus Syndrome or Paroxysmal AV Block): (10%)
    Syncope in the elderly is cardiac until proven otherwise. The palpitations could represent a tachy-brady syndrome, though the specific postprandial timing makes this less likely than a metabolic cause.

  5. Postprandial Hypotension: (5%)
    A common cause of syncope in the elderly where blood pressure drops significantly after eating due to splanchnic blood pooling. However, this usually doesn't present with significant weight loss or headaches.

Recommended Prioritised Tests:

  1. Fingerstick Glucose and Formal Plasma Glucose/Insulin/C-peptide: Ideally captured during an episode or via a supervised 72-hour fast (if fasting symptoms occur) or a mixed-meal test to confirm hypoglycemia.
  2. Plasma Free Metanephrines or 24-hour Urinary Fractionated Metanephrines: To screen for pheochromocytoma given the headache, sweating, and palpitations.
  3. Inflammatory Markers (ESR and CRP): To screen for Giant Cell Arteritis or occult inflammatory/malignant processes.
  4. 12-Lead ECG and Cardiac Telemetry/Holter Monitor: To evaluate for conduction disturbances or arrhythmias.
  5. CT Abdomen/Pelvis: To investigate the weight loss and look for pancreatic lesions (insulinoma) or adrenal masses (pheochromocytoma).

Next Steps: I would immediately check her orthostatic vitals and bedside glucose. If she is currently asymptomatic, the postprandial nature of the event is the strongest lead.

Key Takeaways and Teaching Pearls:

  • Whipple’s Triad is the Entry Point: Once hypoglycemia is documented and reversed by glucose, the workup must pivot immediately to a "critical sample" (Insulin, C-peptide, Proinsulin, β-hydroxybutyrate, and Drug Screen).
  • Postprandial Timing is a Clue: While insulinomas are typically fasting-related, IAS and NIPHS are classically postprandial. In a non-diabetic patient with postprandial "spells," IAS should be high on the differential.
  • The "Reservoir Effect": In IAS, antibodies bind insulin after a meal, preventing it from acting (causing early hyperglycemia), and then release it unpredictably hours later, causing profound, refractory hypoglycemia.
  • Demographics Matter: While most common in East Asian populations, IAS is increasingly recognized globally and can be triggered by common medications containing sulfhydryl or sulfonamide groups (like the thiazide used here).
  • Antibody Persistence: As seen in this patient, IAA titers can remain positive for a long time even after clinical remission. Clinical stability is achieved when the "buffering" capacity of the antibodies no longer leads to massive, synchronized releases of free insulin.
