Q2 What neurological syndrome is described by the weakness?

MODEL_ANS2 The history describes left crural monoparesis, because weakness is confined predominantly to one lower limb without facial or upper-limb involvement. “Paresis” indicates partial weakness, while crural monoplegia or monoparesis refers to involvement of one lower extremity.

Q3 What additional timing detail is essential in a suspected stroke?

MODEL_ANS3 The exact clock time of symptom onset and the last-known-well time should be recorded. It should also be clarified whether the weakness was maximal immediately, evolved continuously over minutes or hours, or progressed stepwise. Kundu differentiates embolic onset over seconds, thrombotic evolution over minutes to hours, and haemorrhage as very sudden and relentlessly progressive.

Q4 How would you classify the evolution of weakness from this history?

MODEL_ANS4 Sudden onset followed by worsening over several hours may represent a stroke in evolution or evolving stroke. A completed stroke is maximal early, whereas an evolving stroke shows gradual or stepwise progression of the neurological deficit.

Q5 Where could an isolated left lower-limb motor deficit localize?

MODEL_ANS5 It may localize to the contralateral parasagittal motor cortex or its subcortical pathways. A right paracentral or anterior cerebral artery territory lesion should be considered because Kundu lists thrombosis of the paracentral artery among the causes of crural monoplegia. Peripheral nerve, root and spinal causes remain differentials until examination is completed.

Q6 What features should be asked to determine whether the vertigo is peripheral or central?

MODEL_ANS6 Ask specifically about relation to head movement or posture, duration, fatigability, nausea, vomiting, nystagmus, hearing loss, tinnitus and ear fullness. Central features include diplopia, dysarthria, dysphagia, ataxia, limb weakness, sensory symptoms, syncope or drop attacks. Alagappan defines vertigo as an illusion of movement and distinguishes labyrinthine disease from vertebral-artery, cerebellar and brainstem causes.

Q7 What is the significance of the absence of tinnitus and hearing loss?

MODEL_ANS7 Their absence makes Ménière disease and other cochleolabyrinthine disorders less typical, though it does not exclude a peripheral vestibular disorder. Vertebrobasilar ischaemia may cause vertigo with diplopia, dysarthria, ataxia or syncope rather than cochlear symptoms.

Q8 Which additional headache questions are essential in this elderly patient?

MODEL_ANS8 Ask whether it was the first or worst-ever headache, thunderclap in onset, maximal within seconds, occipital, associated with exertion, vomiting, loss of consciousness, seizure or neck stiffness. Also ask about scalp tenderness, jaw claudication and constitutional symptoms. Severe abrupt headache, vomiting, impaired consciousness, seizures and meningeal symptoms would increase concern for intracranial or subarachnoid haemorrhage.

Q9 Which posterior-circulation symptoms must be specifically excluded?

MODEL_ANS9 Diplopia, dysarthria, dysphagia, vomiting, gait or truncal ataxia, sudden falls, visual loss and crossed cranial-nerve or limb symptoms should be asked. Alagappan describes vertebrobasilar stroke with dizziness or vertigo, diplopia, drop attacks, dysphagia, dysarthria and pyramidal signs.

Q10 Which vascular risk factors remain incompletely documented?

MODEL_ANS10 Diabetes mellitus, dyslipidaemia, obesity, smoking exposure, previous TIA or stroke, coronary or peripheral arterial disease and family history of premature vascular disease should be explicitly documented. Hypertension, diabetes, smoking, obesity, dyslipidaemia and cardiac disease are major stroke risk factors.

Q11 What history would you take to identify a cardiac embolic source?

MODEL_ANS11 Ask about palpitations, irregular pulse, syncope, rheumatic fever, valvular heart disease, infective endocarditis, recent myocardial infarction, cardiomyopathy and prior anticoagulant use. Atrial fibrillation, mitral stenosis, myocardial infarction and other structural cardiac disorders are recognized embolic sources.

Q12 What previous transient symptoms should be specifically sought?

MODEL_ANS12 Ask about earlier transient unilateral weakness or numbness, speech disturbance, monocular visual loss, diplopia, vertigo, dysarthria, dysphagia, ataxia or drop attacks. Carotid-territory TIAs commonly cause contralateral weakness, sensory loss, aphasia or amaurosis fugax, whereas vertebrobasilar TIAs may cause vertigo and brainstem symptoms.

Q13 How should the hypertension and treatment history be expanded?

MODEL_ANS13 Document previous blood-pressure readings, duration and severity of hypertension, regularity of treatment, reasons for nonadherence, last dose, prescribing physician, adverse effects and evidence of renal, cardiac, retinal or neurological target-organ involvement. A complete history should contain a separate treatment history rather than only naming the medication.

Q14 Which important past and family-history points are missing?

MODEL_ANS14 Drug allergies should be explicitly stated. Ask about previous stroke or TIA, diabetes, dyslipidaemia, coronary disease, arrhythmia, migraine, seizures, malignancy and thrombotic disease. Family history should include stroke, hypertension, diabetes and premature cardiovascular disease. Kundu’s structured history includes past, family, personal, social and treatment histories as distinct components.

Q15 What is the provisional diagnosis from the history alone?

MODEL_ANS15 The provisional diagnosis is an acute cerebrovascular event presenting as left crural monoparesis, possibly an evolving right anterior cerebral or parasagittal cortical infarction, in a patient with poorly controlled hypertension. Intracerebral haemorrhage, posterior-circulation ischaemia with a separate motor lesion, seizure-related Todd paresis, and spinal, root or peripheral-nerve pathology remain differentials until examination and neuroimaging are reviewed. Stroke is defined clinically by an abrupt focal neurological deficit resulting from cerebral vascular pathology.

]Q1 Based on the sudden onset of isolated left lower limb weakness in a known hypertensive patient, what is the most likely anatomical localization for this lesion?
MODEL_ANS1 The most likely localization is the contralateral (right) motor cortex, specifically the medial aspect of the frontal lobe (Anterior Cerebral Artery territory), or it could be a small subcortical lacunar infarct in the internal capsule or corona radiata. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Chapter on Cerebrovascular Disorders).

Q2 Why did you specifically ask about the absence of bladder and bowel dysfunction in this patient?
MODEL_ANS2 To rule out a spinal cord pathology (myelopathy) such as transverse myelitis, anterior spinal artery syndrome, or compressive lesions like cauda equina/conus medullaris syndrome, which frequently present with paraparesis or lower limb weakness accompanied by sphincter dysfunction. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part I, Nervous System).

Q3 What is the significance of the patient's headache and vertigo preceding the limb weakness by a few days?
MODEL_ANS3 In a patient with poorly controlled hypertension, severe headache and vertigo can be prodromal symptoms of a hypertensive crisis, potentially leading to a hypertensive intracerebral hemorrhage or ischemic stroke. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Chapter on Cerebrovascular Disorders).

Q4 You mentioned the patient was irregularly taking Amlodipine and Atenolol. How does medication non-compliance directly contribute to the pathogenesis of her current neurological deficit?
MODEL_ANS4 Chronic, poorly controlled hypertension causes lipohyalinosis of small penetrating blood vessels (increasing the risk of lacunar infarcts) and the formation of Charcot-Bouchard microaneurysms (predisposing to intracerebral hemorrhage). (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Cerebrovascular Disorders).

Q5 Why is it important to confirm the absence of sensory loss or a "sensory level" in this patient?
MODEL_ANS5 A definite sensory level on the trunk strongly suggests a focal spinal cord lesion (e.g., transverse myelitis or spinal cord compression) rather than a cerebral event. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part I, Nervous System).

Q6 The patient experienced weakness while climbing stairs. How does the onset of symptoms help differentiate between an ischemic stroke and a hemorrhagic stroke?
MODEL_ANS6 Hemorrhagic strokes often occur during active exertion or stress (due to sudden spikes in blood pressure), whereas ischemic strokes (particularly thrombotic) frequently occur during rest or sleep. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Cerebrovascular Disorders).

Q7 What further questions would you ask to differentiate between an Anterior Cerebral Artery (ACA) territory infarct and a lacunar (pure motor) infarct?
MODEL_ANS7 I would probe for cortical signs such as behavioral/personality changes, abulia, and cortical sensory loss in the affected limb, which indicate an ACA stroke. A pure motor lacunar stroke usually lacks these cortical and sensory features. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Stroke).

Q8 What is the clinical significance of asking about facial deviation, slurring of speech, and upper limb weakness?
MODEL_ANS8 These symptoms are classically associated with Middle Cerebral Artery (MCA) territory involvement. Their absence helps localize the lesion away from the MCA territory or suggests a very restricted focal lesion. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Cerebrovascular Disorders).

Q9 Why did you ask about a history of fever or recent infection?
MODEL_ANS9 To rule out infectious or post-infectious causes of acute weakness, such as acute transverse myelitis, Guillain-Barré Syndrome (usually bilateral), or a cerebral abscess. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part II, Nervous System).

Q10 How would you classify a "Transient Ischemic Attack" (TIA) based on history, and does this patient fit the criteria?
MODEL_ANS10 A TIA is a transient episode of neurological dysfunction caused by focal brain, spinal cord, or retinal ischemia, without acute infarction, with symptoms typically resolving within 24 hours. Since her weakness has persisted and worsened over a day, it does not fit the criteria for a TIA. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Cerebrovascular Disorders).

Q11 You elicited a negative history of seizures. Why is this a crucial negative in the context of acute focal weakness?
MODEL_ANS11 To rule out Todd's paresis, which is a transient focal weakness that can follow a focal motor seizure and mimic an acute stroke. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part I, Nervous System).

Q12 What is the clinical utility of the Modified Kuppuswamy Scale in this patient's history?
MODEL_ANS12 It helps assess the patient's socioeconomic status, which provides context for her healthcare access, dietary habits, and the likely reason for her irregular compliance with antihypertensive medications. (Reference: General Clinical Methods).

Q13 What is the classic triad of symptoms often seen in raised intracranial pressure, and how many does this patient have?
MODEL_ANS13 The classic triad includes headache, vomiting, and papilledema (or altered sensorium). The patient has a headache but no history of vomiting or altered sensorium, making massive raised ICP less likely but not completely ruled out. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Neurology).

Q14 Why is it necessary to ask about a history of trauma or falls?
MODEL_ANS14 To rule out traumatic brain injury or a chronic/acute subdural hematoma, which can present with gradual or sudden onset focal neurological deficits, especially in older individuals. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Cerebrovascular Disorders).

Q15 If this patient had atrial fibrillation, how would it change your primary differential diagnosis?
MODEL_ANS15 It would make a cardioembolic stroke highly likely, prompting a search for a cardiac source of embolus rather than assuming it is purely a hypertensive/thrombotic event. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Cerebrovascular Disorders).

[PHASE 2 — PHYSICAL EXAMINATION PRESENTATION

]Q1 What neurological syndrome is demonstrated by the motor examination?

MODEL_ANS1 The examination suggests a left-predominant, leg-dominant incomplete hemiparesis, because the left lower limb is most affected, but subtle left upper-limb weakness and pronator drift are also present. It should not be described as isolated crural monoparesis. Kundu defines incomplete hemiplegia as unilateral motor involvement without the full facio-brachio-crural pattern.

Q2 What is the probable anatomical localization?

MODEL_ANS2 The leg-predominant weakness with facial sparing suggests a lesion in the right cerebral hemisphere, particularly the parasagittal motor cortex or adjacent subcortical corticospinal fibres. A right anterior cerebral artery territory lesion is a clinical possibility because the medial motor cortex predominantly represents the contralateral lower limb. However, localization remains provisional because right lower-limb weakness and symmetrical reflex abnormalities were also recorded. Kundu emphasizes that the distribution of limb and cranial-nerve involvement is used to localize hemiplegia.

Q3 What is the significance of the left pronator drift?

MODEL_ANS3 Pronator drift is evidence of subtle pyramidal weakness in the left upper limb. It means that the deficit is not confined to the left leg and supports a mild left hemiparetic syndrome. The arms should be extended with the palms upward and eyes closed; pronation with downward drift suggests corticospinal weakness.

Q4 Why must the recorded muscle power be re-examined?

MODEL_ANS4 Power was grouped under broad joint movements and repeatedly described as “approximately.” Each major movement should be tested separately and compared with the opposite side. Grade 3 denotes movement against gravity but not resistance; grade 4 denotes movement against resistance but weaker than normal; 4− and 4+ may be used to refine grade 4. Kundu and Alagappan recommend systematic muscle-group-wise MRC grading.

Q5 How do you interpret the limb-girth measurements?

MODEL_ANS5 A right-to-left upper-limb difference of 6 cm and lower-limb difference of 3 cm is incompatible with the statement that bulk is normal and symmetrical. The measurements must be repeated at identical distances from fixed bony landmarks, using the olecranon for the upper limb and tibial tuberosity for the lower limb. True differences of this magnitude would require explanation as wasting, oedema, measurement error or pre-existing asymmetry.

Q6 Does normal tone exclude an acute upper motor-neuron lesion?

MODEL_ANS6 No. In the early stage of an acute cerebral lesion, tone may be normal or reduced before spasticity develops. Kundu describes an initial cerebral neural-shock stage with hypotonia and suppression of reflex activity, followed later by recovery of reflexes and tone. Nevertheless, normal tone combined with brisk reflexes and flexor plantar responses makes the examination pattern internally mixed and warrants repetition.

Q7 How should the bilateral 3+ deep-tendon reflexes be interpreted?

MODEL_ANS7 Grade 3+ reflexes are brisk, but symmetrical briskness in isolation does not reliably lateralize a lesion. Their significance depends on asymmetry, spread, clonus, loss of superficial reflexes and an extensor plantar response. A classical established UMN lesion usually produces brisk deep reflexes, diminished superficial reflexes and an extensor plantar response.

Q8 What is the significance of absent ankle jerks bilaterally?

MODEL_ANS8 Bilateral absent ankle jerks are not explained by a unilateral cerebral lesion. They may reflect technical difficulty, age-related change, peripheral neuropathy, bilateral S1 radiculopathy or another lower motor-neuron process. They should be rechecked with reinforcement and correlated with distal vibration, joint-position sensation, muscle wasting and metabolic risk factors. Kundu contrasts brisk reflexes in UMN lesions with diminished or absent reflexes in LMN or peripheral lesions.

Q9 Do bilateral flexor plantar responses exclude an acute stroke?

MODEL_ANS9 They do not completely exclude a very early or small stroke, because the full pyramidal pattern may not yet be established. However, an extensor plantar response is the expected classical sign of a developed corticospinal lesion. Therefore, the flexor responses reduce the certainty of an established UMN syndrome and should be rechecked with proper technique.

Q10 Which cerebellar tests were essential because the patient presented with vertigo?

MODEL_ANS10 Finger–nose testing, rapid alternating movements, rebound phenomenon, heel–knee–shin testing where power permits, truncal stability, nystagmus assessment and observation for a reeling gait should have been recorded. Kundu’s neurological examination specifically includes intention tremor, dysdiadochokinesia, nystagmus, finger–nose testing and gait.

Q11 Why is examination of stance and gait indispensable here?

MODEL_ANS11 Gait could distinguish a circumducting hemiparetic gait from a cerebellar, vestibular, sensory or painful gait. Tandem gait and stance are particularly relevant in vertigo. Kundu includes stance, gait and Romberg testing as mandatory components of the neurological examination and describes circumduction in classical hemiplegic gait.

Q12 What technical error is present in the vibration-sense examination?

MODEL_ANS12 Vibration sense should ideally be tested with a 128-Hz tuning fork, not the 512-Hz fork generally used for hearing tests. The fork should be applied to distal bony prominences with the patient’s eyes closed and the result compared bilaterally. Alagappan notes that 128 Hz persists longer than 512 Hz and is preferred for vibration testing.

Q13 Which cortical sensory test remains important despite otherwise normal sensation?

MODEL_ANS13 Bilateral simultaneous stimulation should be performed to look for sensory extinction or neglect. A patient may perceive stimuli normally when each side is tested separately but fail to perceive the contralateral stimulus during simultaneous stimulation, suggesting a parietal cortical lesion.

Q14 What important vascular examination findings were omitted?

MODEL_ANS14 The pulse must be documented for rate, rhythm, volume, character, arterial-wall condition and pulse deficits. Both carotids should be assessed, including auscultation for bruit, and blood pressure should ideally be compared in both arms. Kundu emphasizes that an irregular pulse from atrial fibrillation, carotid bruit and cardiac murmurs are of major importance in a patient with hemiplegia.

Q15 State the examination diagnosis with appropriate limitations.

MODEL_ANS15 The examination suggests left-predominant, lower-limb-dominant incomplete hemiparesis with preserved higher functions, cranial nerves and sensation, probably due to a right supratentorial corticospinal lesion. A right parasagittal frontal or anterior cerebral artery territory lesion is possible. However, the diagnosis requires repeat examination because of inconsistent limb-girth measurements, mild right lower-limb weakness, symmetrical generalized brisk reflexes, absent ankle jerks, normal tone, flexor plantar responses and missing gait or cerebellar findings. Kundu stresses that localization requires integrated interpretation of power, tone, reflexes, sensation, cranial nerves and gait rather than any single sign.

[PHASE 2 — PHYSICAL EXAMINATION PRESENTATION

]Q1 You recorded the patient's blood pressure but explicitly noted that the pulse was "not clearly documented." In a hypertensive patient presenting with acute neurological deficits, why is the evaluation of the pulse critical?
MODEL_ANS1 The pulse must be evaluated for rate, rhythm, and volume to rule out arrhythmias, particularly atrial fibrillation, which is a major risk factor for cardioembolic stroke. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part I, General Examination).

Q2 You measured the patient's blood pressure as 170/90 mmHg. In the setting of an acute ischemic stroke, what is the physiological rationale behind this reading, and what is the textbook guidance on lowering it immediately?
MODEL_ANS2 Elevated blood pressure in acute stroke is often a compensatory mechanism to maintain cerebral perfusion to the ischemic penumbra. Textbooks advise against rapidly lowering the blood pressure unless it exceeds 220/120 mmHg or thrombolytic therapy is planned, to avoid worsening cerebral ischemia. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Cerebrovascular Disorders).

Q3 You documented muscle bulk as "normal and symmetrical," yet the measurements show the left upper limb is 17 cm and the right is 23 cm (a 6 cm difference). How do you correctly measure limb girth to assess bulk, and what does this discrepancy imply?
MODEL_ANS3 Limb girth should be measured with a measuring tape at a fixed, identical distance from a bony prominence bilaterally (e.g., 10 cm above the olecranon for the arm). A difference of >1-2 cm indicates significant asymmetry, suggesting either wasting on the left or an error in your measurement technique. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part I, Nervous System).

Q4 Despite noting "normal tone," you elicited a positive pronator drift in the left upper limb. What is the clinical significance of a positive pronator drift?
MODEL_ANS4 Pronator drift is a highly sensitive sign of subtle, early, or mild upper motor neuron (UMN) weakness (pyramidal tract lesion), even before objective changes in power or tone become prominent. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Nervous System).

Q5 You graded the left knee extension power as 3/5. According to the Medical Research Council (MRC) grading system, what exactly does a grade of 3/5 signify?
MODEL_ANS5 Grade 3/5 indicates that the muscle can perform active movement against gravity, but not against any applied resistance. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part I, Nervous System).

Q6 You documented the deep tendon reflexes as 3+ (brisk) bilaterally, yet the plantar responses are flexor bilaterally. How do you explain this discrepancy in the context of an acute unilateral UMN lesion?
MODEL_ANS6 In an acute UMN lesion, there is initially a phase of "cerebral shock" where reflexes may be diminished and the plantar response remains flexor. The presence of 3+ reflexes bilaterally with flexor plantars likely reflects a pre-existing generalized brisk reflex baseline (perhaps due to age or anxiety) or bilateral pyramidal tract hyper-excitability, whereas the Babinski sign has not yet developed on the paralyzed side. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part I, Nervous System).

Q7 The ankle jerks were absent bilaterally in this 66-year-old female. What are the common physiological and pathological reasons for this isolated finding?
MODEL_ANS7 Bilateral absence of ankle jerks can be a physiological finding in the elderly. Pathologically, it often points to a pre-existing sensory or sensorimotor peripheral neuropathy, such as diabetic or hypertensive polyneuropathy. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part I, Nervous System).

Q8 You stated that the nasolabial folds are symmetrical and CN VII is intact. If this patient had a classic Middle Cerebral Artery (MCA) territory stroke, what facial nerve findings would you expect?
MODEL_ANS8 An MCA stroke typically involves the motor cortex supplying the face, leading to a contralateral upper motor neuron (UMN) facial palsy. This presents as weakness of the lower half of the face (flattened nasolabial fold and drooping of the angle of the mouth), with sparing of the upper face (frontalis) due to bilateral cortical innervation. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Nervous System).

Q9 The weakness in this patient is predominantly in the left lower limb (power 3/5 to 4-/5) compared to the left upper limb (power 4/5) and the face (spared). Which specific vascular territory does this localize to?
MODEL_ANS9 This localizes to the right Anterior Cerebral Artery (ACA) territory, which supplies the medial surface of the frontal and parietal lobes, controlling the contralateral lower limb. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Cerebrovascular Disorders).

Q10 You documented testing vibration sense with a 512-Hz tuning fork. Why is this technically incorrect for the neurological evaluation of the posterior column?
MODEL_ANS10 A 128-Hz tuning fork must be used for testing vibration sense because its lower frequency provides the optimal amplitude and prolonged vibration necessary to stimulate the Pacinian corpuscles. A 512-Hz tuning fork is used for hearing tests (Weber and Rinne). (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part I, Nervous System).

Q11 Why is it crucial to test cerebellar signs and gait in a patient presenting with both acute vertigo and acute limb weakness?
MODEL_ANS11 Vertigo combined with focal weakness raises the suspicion of a posterior circulation stroke (vertebrobasilar territory) affecting the brainstem or cerebellum. Evaluating cerebellar signs (like ataxia or dysmetria) helps differentiate between an anterior and posterior circulation event. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Cerebrovascular Disorders).

Q12 You noted that two-point discrimination and stereognosis were normal. Which lobe of the brain is primarily responsible for processing these cortical sensations?
MODEL_ANS12 The parietal lobe, specifically the primary somatosensory cortex and the somatosensory association cortex, is responsible for processing cortical sensations. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part I, Nervous System).

Q13 In your cardiovascular and general examination, you did not mention auscultating the carotids. Why is this an essential step in this particular case?
MODEL_ANS13 Auscultating for a carotid bruit is essential to screen for carotid artery stenosis, which is a major cause of atherothrombotic stroke or transient ischemic attacks. (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part I, Cardiovascular System).

Q14 You mentioned that the tone was normal. In a typical UMN lesion progressing over time, how do you expect tone to change from the acute phase to the chronic phase?
MODEL_ANS14 In the acute phase (cerebral shock), tone is characteristically flaccid (hypotonia). Over days to weeks, it evolves into hypertonia, specifically spasticity (clasp-knife type). (Reference: Kundu, Bedside Clinics in Medicine, Part I, Nervous System).

Q15 You noted the absence of Kernig's and Brudzinski's signs. What do these signs test for, and why is their absence clinically relevant here?
MODEL_ANS15 These are signs of meningeal irritation. Their absence helps rule out subarachnoid hemorrhage or meningoencephalitis, which can occasionally present with headache, vomiting, and focal neurological deficits. (Reference: Alagappan, Manual of Practical Medicine, Nervous System).
